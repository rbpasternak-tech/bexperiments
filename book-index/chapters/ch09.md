**CHAPTER 9**

**"Vibe Coding" and Shadow AI**

*WORKING DRAFT — February 2026*

Most Chief Innovation Officers in law firms are, at best, Chief Marginal Improvement Officers.

That is a deliberately provocative statement, but it captures something real. The most significant innovations in how legal work is done are not emerging from innovation teams. They are emerging from practicing lawyers who understand their work deeply enough to see where technology can transform it, and who now have the tools to act on that insight without waiting for institutional permission.

This chapter is about what happens when lawyers start building. It is about vibe coding, the practice of creating software through conversational prompts to AI rather than through traditional programming. It is about shadow AI, the use of AI tools outside official firm channels. And it is about what firms should do in response: not prohibition, which does not work, but a framework that captures the energy of individual innovation while managing the risks it creates.

The term vibe coding originates with AI researcher Andrej Karpathy, who used it to describe a new relationship with code: one focused on intent and iteration rather than on understanding every line. For lawyers, vibe coding represents something more fundamental. It is the collapse of a barrier that has existed since the first law firm bought a computer: the barrier between the people who understand the work and the people who build the tools. \[CITATION NEEDED: Andrej Karpathy, original vibe coding post\]

## **The Democratisation of Building**

Until very recently, if a lawyer identified a workflow that could be automated, a process that could be streamlined, or a tool that would make their practice more efficient, they had one option: ask someone else to build it. They could submit a request to the innovation team. They could lobby for budget. They could wait.

That wait is over. Tools now exist that allow a lawyer with no programming background to describe what they want and receive working software. Claude Code, Anthropic's coding agent, can build applications through conversation. Claude Cowork takes this further, removing even the need to understand code: a lawyer can describe a workflow in plain language and receive a functional tool. Base44, acquired by Wix for $80 million after just six months of operation by a single developer, represents the platform approach to no-code creation. Manus and similar tools offer yet another path.

The implications are significant. A partner can now prototype a solution in an evening. An associate can build a tool over a weekend that addresses a specific pain point in their practice. A knowledge lawyer can create a workflow that captures institutional expertise in a form that is immediately usable. None of these people need to understand programming. They need to understand their work.

This is why the chapter opens with the observation about innovation officers. True and meaningful innovation in law firms happens when practicing lawyers, the people who understand the substance of the work, are empowered to build. Innovation teams play a critical role in infrastructure, governance, and scaling, but the creative spark, the identification of what needs to exist, comes from practice.

## **Shadow AI: The Invisible Adoption Curve**

Shadow AI is the use of AI tools outside of official firm channels. A lawyer uses a personal ChatGPT account to draft a memo. An associate uploads a contract to Claude to get a first-pass review. A partner uses an AI research tool they discovered independently, without going through the firm's procurement process.

This is happening at every firm. It is happening right now. The question is not whether it is occurring but whether the firm knows about it, and whether the firm has created conditions that make it unnecessary.

From a risk perspective, shadow AI creates genuine concerns. Files uploaded to personal accounts may be stored in ways inconsistent with firm policy. Consumer-grade AI tools may train on user inputs. Lawyers may inadvertently waive attorney-client privilege by processing confidential information through unsanctioned platforms. And as discussed in the previous chapter, the proliferation of agent-based tools creates the risk that lawyers grant AI systems broad access to their computers and networks without understanding the security implications.

But from an innovation perspective, shadow AI represents something valuable: lawyers solving their own problems. When a lawyer circumvents the official process to use an AI tool, they are demonstrating initiative, identifying a need that the firm's approved tools do not meet, and acting on it. That energy should be captured, not crushed.

## **The Two Paths**

Firms have two fundamental options when confronted with vibe coding and shadow AI.

The first is to ignore it or prohibit it. This approach has a superficial appeal: it appears to manage risk by eliminating the activity. But prohibition does not eliminate the activity. It drives it underground. Talented lawyers who want to build and innovate will either do it secretly, incurring all of the risks that shadow AI creates, or they will leave for firms that give them the space to create.

The second is to embrace it within a framework. This means acknowledging that lawyers are building, creating supervised environments where they can do so safely, and establishing processes to evaluate, improve, and scale the most promising innovations.

The framework approach requires several elements. First, a sandbox environment where lawyers can experiment freely without risking client data or firm security. This can be a dedicated instance of a coding tool, a private environment with synthetic data, or a governed workspace within the firm's infrastructure. The key is that it exists, that lawyers know about it, and that it is easy to use.

Second, it requires expert review before anything moves from prototype to production. A tool built by a lawyer in an evening may solve a real problem, but it is unlikely to meet the firm's standards for security, data governance, error handling, or scalability. The innovation team's role is not to gatekeep but to elevate: to take a promising prototype and make it enterprise-grade.

Third, it requires a pathway from prototype to product. The most effective approach is often not to maintain the lawyer-built tool indefinitely but to migrate its functionality into supported infrastructure. If a lawyer builds a contract analysis workflow using Claude Code, the firm might take the concept and implement it within its preferred platform, whether that is Legora, Harvey, DeepJudge, or another tool with the infrastructure to support ongoing maintenance and updates. Alternatively, the firm might share the concept with a vendor and ask them to incorporate it into their product roadmap. The lawyer gets the benefit immediately. The firm gets a supported, scalable version over time.

## **Build, Buy, or Rent: Revisited**

The rise of vibe coding forces a reconsideration of the build-versus-buy decision discussed in Chapter 7\. The traditional framing assumed that building was expensive and slow, requiring dedicated development resources. Vibe coding makes building fast and cheap, but it does not make it sustainable.

A tool built by a lawyer using conversational AI is, in effect, a startup with one client: the firm. The lawyer who built it will not maintain it indefinitely. They will get bored, get busy with billable work, or move on. The tool will break when the underlying model changes, when the firm's systems are updated, or when the use case evolves. Without ongoing maintenance, the tool becomes a liability rather than an asset.

This is why the answer, as discussed in Chapter 7, is often to rent: to treat lawyer-built tools as prototypes that prove a concept, and then to migrate that concept into commercially maintained products. Harvey, Legora, and similar platforms are maintained by teams of engineers serving hundreds of clients. They will update the product. They will fix bugs. They will ensure compatibility with evolving firm infrastructure. A solo lawyer's weekend project, however brilliant, cannot offer that.

There is a meaningful exception. If the tool represents a genuine strategic advantage, something proprietary that differentiates the firm's practice, it may warrant dedicated development resources to maintain and scale. But for most lawyer-built tools, which tend to be clever workflow improvements rather than competitive differentiators, the migration path is the right one.

## **The Open Question: Will Firms Need Vendors?**

A harder question is emerging: as vibe coding tools become more powerful and more accessible, will firms continue to invest in external legal AI platforms?

Some contributors to this book believe the answer is clearly yes. The argument is that platforms like Harvey and Legora offer integrated capabilities, enterprise security, ongoing maintenance, and a breadth of functionality that no collection of lawyer-built tools can replicate. The million-dollar annual licence fee, however significant, buys infrastructure that the firm could not build and maintain for less.

Others see a future where firms shift investment from external platforms to internal capability. Teams composed of lawyer subject-matter experts paired with engineers, equipped with tools like Claude Code, could build custom solutions tailored precisely to the firm's needs. In this model, the external platform becomes less necessary, and the renewal conversation becomes more difficult.

The most likely outcome is a hybrid. Firms will maintain platform subscriptions for the broad, general-purpose capabilities they provide. But an increasing share of innovation will happen at the edges, driven by lawyers who understand their practice and can now build for it. The firms that thrive will be those that create the infrastructure to support both paths, managing the relationship between centrally procured platforms and decentralised, lawyer-driven innovation.

## **Recognising and Rewarding Innovation**

If firms want lawyers to innovate, they must recognise and reward the behaviour. This is a challenge for a profession built around the billable hour.

A lawyer who spends an evening building a tool that saves their practice group hundreds of hours has created significant value. Under a traditional billable hour model, that evening was unbillable. Worse, the tool they built may reduce the firm's revenue by making future work faster. The incentive structure actively discourages the behaviour the firm wants to encourage.

Some firms have begun to address this. Ropes and Gray's programme granting first-year associates four hundred hours of AI credit is one approach. \[CITATION NEEDED: Ropes & Gray AI credit programme details\] Others are creating innovation bonus pools, giving billable credit for time spent building tools, or featuring lawyer-innovators in internal communications.

Recognition may matter more than financial reward. The lawyers most inclined to build are often motivated by the satisfaction of solving problems, by the recognition of their peers, and by the opportunity to shape how their practice operates. Firms that celebrate successful innovations, that share them across the organisation, and that give credit where it is due will find that innovation begets innovation.

But financial incentives matter too. A lawyer who spends significant time on non-billable innovation and sees it reflected negatively in their annual review will stop innovating. Firms must ensure that their compensation and evaluation structures do not penalise the behaviour they want to encourage. This may mean adjusting billable hour targets for recognised innovators, creating alternative evaluation criteria for lawyers involved in tool building, or establishing innovation as a formal component of the path to partnership.

## **The Cannibalization Question**

Vibe coding raises an uncomfortable question for some firms: what happens when internally built tools compete with the firm's own revenue-generating services?

The most concrete example is eDiscovery. Several large firms maintain significant eDiscovery practices that generate substantial revenue. If a lawyer builds an AI-powered document review tool, or if a general-purpose AI platform begins to offer capabilities that overlap with dedicated eDiscovery tools, the firm faces a tension between innovation and revenue protection.

This is not a new tension. Every industry that has been disrupted by technology has faced the cannibalization question. The answer from other industries is consistent: if you do not cannibalise your own revenue, someone else will. The question is not whether AI will transform eDiscovery and other technology-adjacent legal services. It is whether the firm will lead that transformation or be displaced by it.

The practical approach is awareness and planning. Firms should understand where their revenue is vulnerable to AI-driven disruption, have a strategy for evolving those services, and ensure that internal innovation efforts are coordinated with commercial strategy. A lawyer building an AI-powered review workflow should be supported, but the firm should also be thinking about how to incorporate that capability into its commercial offering rather than letting it undermine existing revenue without replacement.

## **Looking Ahead**

Vibe coding and shadow AI are not problems to be solved. They are signals of a fundamental shift in how legal work will be done. The lawyers who are building today are the vanguard of a profession that will increasingly expect its practitioners to be creators as well as advisors.

Firms that respond to this shift with curiosity and structure, that create the environments, incentives, and governance frameworks to support lawyer-driven innovation, will capture an enormous amount of value. Firms that respond with prohibition and control will lose their most talented and ambitious people to those that do not.

The next chapter turns to the relationships that make all of this work: the partnerships between in-house legal teams, outside counsel, and the technology vendors that serve them both.
