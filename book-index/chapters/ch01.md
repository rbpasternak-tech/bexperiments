# **Chapter 1**

## **What’s Changing and Why it Matters**

### **Introduction**

For decades the legal profession has been proceeding along a familiar path. While new technologies have come and gone, each promising large-scale disruption, the scenery is still recognisable. Law firms bill by the hour. In-house teams are often seen as cost centres. Partnership structures remain largely intact. Technology within firms is usually managed by centralized innovation teams. Technology for in-house teams means relying on the tools available in the rest of the business rather than legal-specific solutions. 

And yet, something feels different.

The ground is shifting. The billable hour remains but clients are specifically asking about a definition of value that goes beyond the hours that a task takes, and are unwilling to pay for tasks that could and should be supported by technology. Alternative fee arrangements (AFAs) and effective fee arrangements (EFAs) are on the rise. In-house legal teams are leveraging opportunities to become more strategic and to implement legal operations teams or disciplines. Private capital investment in law is driving a review of partnership structures. Lawyers are independently ‘vibe coding’ their own solutions instead of waiting for central innovation or IT teams to solve their problems. A lawyer can now type in the problem they want to solve, develop a solution right then and there, and refine it with their problem solving skills and subject matter expertise \- all without writing a single line of traditional code. 

The pressure points have moved. The desire for change is coming not from legal teams or from innovators necessarily, but from the business world as a whole. In-house legal departments are deploying the same AI platforms as their outside counsel, and can now benchmark panel firm efficiency against their own capabilities or \- in some instances \- replace them. Investment into technology is higher than ever before as people expect its use. The results are different. Technology is delivering on things which have previously been overpromised and underdelivered in other hype cycles and all done in natural language that reduces the barrier to entry for legal practitioners.  This book is being written because with Generative AI something *has* changed, even if much of the system around it has not yet fully responded.

This chapter is about that change: what is happening, why it is happening now, and why it matters for anyone working in or alongside the legal profession. We will examine where we are today, where we might be going, and how we get there, via the lenses of evolutions in technology, people, and policy. 

### **The AI landscape in law**

#### How we got here

Generative AI did not arrive in a vacuum. By the time large language models entered mainstream professional consciousness, the legal sector was already under sustained pressure to do more with less. For a large part, AI has accelerated trends that were already underway, rather than creating them from scratch. But the way in which it is being used and responding to those broader pressures differs from previous technologies like blockchain, no-code, or even the first round of non-generative artificial intelligence solutions.

To understand the current moment, it helps to see how we arrived here. \[For a more in-depth review of the history and workings of AI, see Chapter 2.\] The application of AI in law has evolved through distinct phases: Natural Language Processing (NLP) in the early 2000s enabled “understanding” text for tasks like document review. Machine Learning (ML) in the mid-2010s brought patterns recognition for data-driven insights. The explosive entry on the scene of Large Language Models (LLMs) and Generative AI in late 2022 introduced automated text (and, later, image and video) generation via natural language prompting. And now, agentic AI and beyond promises autonomous, multi-step goal execution. Each wave has built upon the last, but the current iteration represents something qualitatively different in its accessibility and capability. 

#### The current landscape

What is new is not simply the availability of technology, but the ways that it can be used. A significant barrier to adoption in the past has been in lawyers being able to find the time to use new technology. A new software system inevitably meant mastering new processes, new menus and user interfaces, and having to fit the way that lawyers work into the strictures of the particular tool. These tools also existed as a series of point solutions instead of an integrated end-to-end workflow, despite the best efforts of vendors, meaning that users might have to jump through multiple interfaces all with their own ways of being accessed and used. With Generative AI, lawyers can suddenly ask the technology for what they want in natural language. For senior lawyers in particular, the way that they draft their instructions (or “prompts”) in Generative AI systems is no different than how they would give instructions to juniors. This has been both a boon and a curse to the rapid expansion of Generative AI. It has allowed people to pick up the tools and use them with little training, but has meant that there has been less traction with more sophisticated uses of the technology. \[Insert articles about usage and Gen AI pilots not taking off\].

In the first few years of the rise of Generative AI, many tools fell into exactly the same pitfalls as previous technologies. The legal-specific AI tools started out as simple chatbot-style environments where they were good at completing singular tasks. As the requirements for complexity grew, the tools added new features: standardized tabular review, workflows, collaborative spaces. These all existed within environments that included new menus and new processes that went beyond simply typing in what you want the tool to do. For the most part, usage continued to focus around the simple chatbot environment. Lawyers wanted to do more but did not have time to learn the new menus without support from innovation teams or, for in-house teams, their IT teams or panel firms.

Enter agents. Generative AI up to the point of the introduction of agents was a series of singular tasks that the technology could achieve. Some people had built specific workflows but these tended to be templated prompts rather than true agents. For the purposes of this book, the term “agent” refers to an autonomous software system powered by Generative AI that can navigate between different environments in order to achieve a task. Let us take due diligence as a good example of the difference agents make. In our example Company A is considering purchasing Company B. Company B has instructed Roscoe, Jasper & Mills LLP (RJM) to conduct a review of the status of Company A to highlight any potential legal risks before purchase. RJM is going to use its Generative AI solution to support this due diligence. The corporate practice will coordinate the due diligence but will work with other practices who will be looking for potential issues in their specialist areas, such as employment or real estate. In the earliest examples of AI-enabled due diligence, a common flow might look like the following:

1. Download the relevant documents regarding Company A from a data room   
2. Create a new workspace within the Generative AI tool  
3. Upload the data room documents into the tool  
4. Manually create separate tabular review of the documents for each practice (e.g. Corporate, Employment, etc), entering in the relevant prompts for each column  
5. Select which documents belong to which table  
6. Manually review the outputs by comparing to the original documents  
7. Ask the Generative AI tool to produce a first draft due diligence report summarizing the main issues

It is easy to see the opportunities for losing engagement from lawyers at each step. Without the relevant knowledge, they would need to know how to: upload documents into the tool, create new workspaces, set up a tabular review, enter in the appropriate prompts, and ask in a separate section to produce a written report. Suddenly this doesn’t look as simple as just telling the technology what you want it to do in plain language. With the assistance of an agent, however, this process can be as simple as:

1. A lawyer from RJM writes a prompt that they wish to conduct a due diligence process and need a table of issues that creates a due diligence report  
2. The agent completes the relevant steps to create a workspace, categorise the documents, create the tables, write the prompts, and draft the due diligence report

This is the second wave of adoption for lawyers, where the tools move from being able to complete discrete tasks to being able to navigate an environment of tools to accomplish outcomes. This does assume that the tool has appropriate access to the various systems like the data room or document repository and that the foundational elements of the AI in terms of security and deployment have been met, but the process for use has become much easier. 

In addition, lawyers are no longer only hearing about AI tools from innovation teams or conferences. They are seeing colleagues use it. They are being asked by partners whether entire processes can be automated end-to-end. They are hearing clients ask what AI is doing behind the scenes, and whether it is reflected in price, speed, quality, or scope. It is not just law that is being affected but the entire world.

How are lawyers currently using AI in law? The pace of change makes it difficult to anchor discussion in any specific tools. Any list of platforms or use cases risks being out of date before the ink dries. But what matters more than the specific use cases is the pattern: experimentation is happening everywhere, often without central coordination, and often driven by individuals trying to solve immediate problems under time and cost pressure.

The results reveal gaps that are as important as the strengths. Technical legal expertise is consistently assumed rather than highlighted. This gap between current focus and desired knowledge suggests a profession that has moved beyond “whether” to adopt AI and is now grappling with “how” to deploy it effectively, and following quickly on the heels of that, how to calculate time and cost savings. 

### **Why is change happening: the people**

One way of understanding what is changing is to look not at technology first, but at people. AI is empowering individuals and sole practitioners in a way that has not previously been possible. The ability to produce a complex output with AI means that smaller firms can go up against firms that have previously set themselves apart by being able to throw large numbers of people against problems. It means that in-house teams can do the work (or at least, some of the work) that previously panel firms would have completed. It means that individual lawyers can create solutions by “vibe coding” that would have only been possible in the past by leveraging an innovation team or similar support function.

\[Expound on several categories of people set out above\]

In-house legal departments have become increasingly sophisticated. Major corporations are now deploying the same AI platforms as their outside counsel (such as Harvey, and other competitor products), and using them to build internal capabilities. AI is arguably much easier to deploy in-house, where the types of activities being undertaken are more repeatable and consistent. Consider an AI platform that is designed to review a contract against a playbook, i.e. a guide containing the rules, preferred clauses, fallback positions and limits on acceptable negotiations for a legal team. In an in-house team, that platform has to consider a single playbook against each of the contracts. In a law firm, the playbook used depends on the practice, the client, and \- in global firms \- the office. Deployment and use becomes much more complex. 

This represents a fundamental shift in what clients value and how they want to receive it. When both sides have access to the same tools, the differentiator becomes the knowledge and judgement that sits on top of them. There are now countless instances of clients giving their law firms a “first pass” review that has been conducted by AI and just asking the panel firm to confirm or amend the output. There is still reliance on the panel firm as they can provide their expertise gleaned from conducting many of the same types of activity for other clients and, of course, they are insured should anything go wrong. But updating a first pass does not take anywhere near as long as providing advice from first principles and in a profession that is built on the billable hour, this means that clients are expecting savings. The same thing is happening within the in-house world, where other departments are using AI to give legal guidance on an outcome.

In addition to clients, the workforce of lawyers themselves are driving change. Some no longer want to or feel that they can conduct certain types of work manually. There is an expectation that the team you are joining will already use Generative AI and that this will reduce the amount of repetitive administrative work that lawyers have to undertake. A lot of the push in this area is coming from juniors who are joining law firms and expect to encounter the same technologies they use in their daily life, though there is also drive from more senior lawyers seeking to save money by lowering their reliance on junior lawyers, and those who are demanding access to AI precisely because they, in turn, are being pushed by their clients.

The other people driving this change are those in leadership. In firms, law firm leaders sensing the direction of travel are making the use of AI a fundamental part of lawyer development \[examples of AI training programs\] or driving AI usage through a range of means \[10000 prompts example\]. Business leaders, who have already been asking in-house teams to be more efficient for years, are asking for more speed and more capability from their lawyers.

This matters because change does not happen magically. Change is driven from pressure. And the pressure to be more efficient is now coming from multiple directions at once. Yet the question of what efficiency *actually* means remains unresolved. If a task that once took ten hours now takes two, does the lawyer do five times as many tasks? Or does the organization need fewer lawyers? How should the law firm charge for that work? The honest answer is that nobody knows yet. This uncertainty creates anxiety across the profession, but it is a question that certainly cannot be ignored.

### **Why change is happening: the economic reality**

If there is a single thread running through almost every conversation about change in law, it is cost. In-house legal teams are being squeezed. They are being asked to move closer to the business, to be more strategic, and to demonstrate value in ways that go beyond technical accuracy. At the same time, their budgets are under scrutiny, often more intensely than other professional functions.

That pressure is shared outward. External legal spend is interrogated while panels are being reviewed and diversified. The question is no longer simply “are you good lawyers?” but “are you the \*right\* lawyers, at the \*right\* price, delivering the \*right\* outcomes?” Over all of this looms a single monolith that has so far remained unmoved by the advancement of years: the billable hour. It is no secret that lawyers bill by the hour (or by six-minute segments if you want to be exact). There has always been a tension between this billing model and the rapid advancement of technologies which increase the speed of tasks. It is one that the legal profession has for the most part navigated through without changing the core model. But it exposes a deep tension at the heart of the traditional law firm model. If lawyers are paid by the hour, why would efficiency ever be prioritised? The answer to this is because they have to. This is starting to happen. For the first time, we are seeing the foundations of the billable hour begin to crumble.

Alternative fee arrangements have become more common, but are still far from the norm. Fixed fees, capped fees, and blended rates appear to promise predictability. At the same time, some clients are becoming more sophisticated in how they buy legal services, requiring secondments or innovation projects to be bundled into fee arrangements, effectively shifting risk and experimentation onto their advisors. Some clients have gone further, implementing credit systems that tie fees to demonstrated efficiency gains. If a firm fails to deliver innovation projects or secondments as agreed, the client can claw back a portion of fees at year-end. Others are asking firms to demonstrate the “delts” \- the difference between what the work cost before AI and what it should cost now. Firms must be able to adequately demonstrate this delta (and understand what it means for the business model) because the era of trusting that outside counsel will pass on efficiency gains voluntarily appears to be at an end for some. 

All these factors have led to much more discussion of “value”. This movement makes sense. Consider a due diligence exercise like in our example above. If the due diligence exercise used to take 10 hours and now only takes one, then RJM is going to lose nine-tenths of its revenue for the exercise \[singular or plural for a LLP?\]. To bolster that revenue they could now do more matters of this type, but there are only so many company acquisitions happening at once. On the other hand, what is the value to Company B of completing a purchase of a company faster? It’s not just the reduced legal spend which are, if we are honest, a necessary evil for the commercial gain of purchasing the company. The value to them is how much quicker they can start trading with their newly purchased Company A. Clearly that is also worth something.

So how do we reach a middle ground? Value based pricing seeks to find a compromise between the traditional hours model and the benefits to the client of more efficient work, while still recognising the time and expertise (and the insurance protection) of the law firm. Liken it to a mechanic who might charge a premium price for fixing a fault in a car. The fix might only take them ten minutes, but you are not paying for their time. You are paying for the years of expertise that allowed them to solve your problem in ten minutes, meaning you are back on the road much faster. That is where the pricing of legal matters are moving as AI becomes more prevalent.

Changing existing processes and working practices takes time, however. It is a famous cartoon in the legal profession that shows a pair of individuals pushing a cart with square wheels while a helpful individual offers them a round one, the pair claiming they don’t have the time to stop pushing. It can sometimes feel like this in the legal profession. But layer on top of this is a growing unwillingness from clients to pay for work that feels non-billable: training, internal inefficiencies, or duplicated effort. This is a difficult quandary for law firms who have to incentivise their lawyers to experiment and optimise their workflows for efficiency while clients are already not paying for this experimentation. 

This is the other facet to the billable hour that goes beyond how clients are billed: the billable hour is still the primary metric for rewarding lawyers. This generally translates into lawyers hitting a certain number of hours in order to qualify for a bonus. Much like with the billable hour method of charging, there has been some change or amendment to this core structure in recent years, with the introduction of forms of contribution hours that might count to a target such as “innovation hours” or “knowledge hours”. But the core model has largely remained unchanged. Why  There is movement here too though, with firms making AI usage part of the appraisal \[example\] and even the bonus \[example\]. For now, however, the result is a system that publicly celebrates change while privately rewarding continuity.  

### Time, transparency, and the metrics problem

Charging or rewarding time is not the only consideration. Expectations have changed. Clients now simply expect answers faster. Lawyers expect tools to remove friction. Everyone feels busier, even when technology promises efficiency. This is because the expectation is not incorporeal \[word?\], it has to be shown. Clients \- whether internal or external \- increasingly want transparency. Clients want to know what they are paying for, how long it will take, and what they will get. Firms want flexibility, discretion, and protection against risk. The way of managing this transparency has been through the use of metrics. While metrics are supposed to bridge that gap, they often reinforce old behaviours instead. Likewise, users of these technologies want their own metrics about usage. 

This has forced more conversations within the profession focused on what actually counts as a valuable key performance indicator (KPI). Hours billed are easy to measure, but they are a poor proxy for value in a world where pricing is more like the mechanic example above. Likewise, number of prompts in an AI system doesn’t guarantee good usage. Innovation contributions, process improvements, and collaboration are harder to quantify, and therefore easier to ignore. Even where clients use alternative fee arrangements, there is still the expectation of a series of narratives showing the activities undertaken by the lawyers. This is understandable; clients want value for money and in a system where hours has been the primary metric then seeing what was done during those hours is a logical request. The problem is how to keep that level of transparency when pricing on value. 

Does the law firm showcase their years of expertise? Do clients start to rank their firms with net promoter scores? Do we start to see law firm comparison sites \[Jamie Tso example\] where clients can choose the firm based on the best price? Does the world become more reliant on the success of individual practitioners of law rather than the firm as a whole? These questions remain unresolved, but they can no longer be avoided.

### Why change is happening: competition, regulation, and new market dynamics

Cost pressure alone does not explain the scale of change. Competition has intensified, not just between law firms, but between entirely different models of legal service delivery. In England and Wales, alternative business structures (ABSs) have been in existence since \[ \], allowing non-lawyers to own and invest in legal businesses. In the United States, regulatory change has been slower and more fragmented, but experimentation is increasing. While the initial promise of ABSs was to revolutionise the market in England and Wales, the effect has mostly been felt at the fringes of the core legal market. What it has done is cause clients to diversify their panels; what was previously a collection of law firms will now almost always include either an ABS or a law firm who is able to conduct ABS-style work, which tends to be higher volume work with consistent pricing. What it has proved is that clients are more willing to look beyond the traditional partnership model if it delivers value. 

The change in the US however comes at a different time. Some have suggested that with the use of AI, “the unauthorized practice of law may die with a whimper not a bang” \[Damien \- need to ask for permission\]. The proliferation of automated workflows means that tasks once requiring lawyer oversight are now performed by software, with a paralegal, perhaps, pushing a button. Is this the practice of law? The question is increasingly difficult to answer, and perhaps increasingly irrelevant. What matters is that work is moving, often invisibly, away from traditional providers. Look at the conveyancing market, where the majority of work is completed by individuals who are not qualified lawyers. For work that is repeatable and subject to strict rules with little scope for complex interpretation, it is easy to see why clients might not care if the person “pushing the button” is a lawyer or not.

With the diversification of panels, new entrants are more specialised. The result is not simply more competition, but *different* competition, forcing law firms and legal teams to articulate what makes them distinct. Large firms that once competed on their ability to staff matters with dozens of associates now find that advantage eroding. Smaller, more agile competitors can produce comparable output with AI assistance, at lower cost.

Perhaps most significant is the role of legal technology vendors themselves. Platforms that began as tools are increasingly hiring for Big Law lawyers, building client relationships, selling to in-house teams, and developing the expertise to deliver legal work directly. They already have the software; they are acquiring the knowledge; what remains is regulatory permission. Many of these legal technology AI companies have multi-million dollar valuations and it is hard to see how that valuation can be justified if they remain only technology vendors who sell into law firms. Whether these vendors become law firms, acquire law firms, or create new hybrid structures remains to be seen, but there is a clear direction of travel, with one observer calling these vendors “the biggest Trojan Horse in legal.”

### A possible future

The legal profession finds itself at a point where technology, people, and policy are all shifting at once. And it is not just in legal, but in every single profession. None of these changes can be understood in isolation. Generative AI matters not because it is novel, but because it collides with existing pressures in a system already stretched thin. Movement is beginning but the real question is where we are going.

What might the future look like? There are a number of options where the legal profession could end up but it is helpful to look at the most extreme versions. This is an example we will return to throughout which can be best described as “legal streaming” or “law as a service” (LaaS). Consider a world where law is more akin to the world of streaming services. Law firms, legal publishers, sole practitioners, and other knowledge providers “broadcast” their services into platform providers. They might do this from their own environments or by purchasing a “box” through which these services can be streamed. Clients can then subscribe to the services they want, and embed them into their own systems as needed. This means a client who has to do a due diligence exercise might activate their “due diligence agent”. The parts of this agentic workflow might use RJM’s mergers & acquisitions law “channel” for their corporate assessment of the risks of the purchase, while using another firm’s employment law “channel” for the employment aspects and yet another’s real estate “channel”. The agent would navigate between these specialists to give a holistic overview of the potential legal issues. Playbooks, precedents, deal analytics, and advisory services flow through a single interface, accessible on demand. 

How do you prepare for this future? We have already established that change is happening quickly and that we don’t know exactly what the profession will look like, but if you prepare for this future then the work will not be wasted. There is no downside to understanding where your data is, what the single points of truth are, and whether it is clean and structured. You will not regret taking the time to understand the key processes and tasks that your organisation undertakes, and where those processes might be made more efficient. And you will see benefits immediately for training your people to think differently about their work, to be curious and aware of whether the things they are doing can be done more efficiently and/or effectively.

As we continue along the path, the next chapters will explore how these pressures play out in practice: how individuals respond, how organisations attempt to regain control, and how governance, incentives, and culture shape what is possible.

A key point to remember is that the ground has moved. Standing still is no longer neutral. And the decisions made in response to this moment will shape not just how law is practised, but who gets to practise it, and on what terms.
