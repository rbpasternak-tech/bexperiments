**CHAPTER 6**

**Training, Engagement, and Adoption**

*WORKING DRAFT — February 2026*

There is no innovation without adoption, and it takes a village.

This chapter is about what happens after the tools are selected, the strategy is set, and the infrastructure is in place. It is about getting lawyers to actually use the technology, to change how they work, and to sustain that change over time. It is also about what happens before any tool is deployed: how the profession trains its next generation in a world where AI has eliminated many of the tasks that junior lawyers once cut their teeth on.

The conversation about training, engagement, and adoption is one the authors know well. It is the daily work of innovation teams in law firms, and it is one of the most difficult problems in legal technology. A tool can be brilliant. The strategy can be sound. The business case can be compelling. But none of it matters if lawyers do not use it and \- just as importantly \- use it well. Being *useful* and being *used* are two very different things. And lawyers, by training and temperament, are not natural early adopters. They are time-poor, trained to identify risk, and rewarded for caution. They have also been promised transformative technology before, and they remember when it disappointed.

This chapter addresses training across multiple levels, from law schools to leadership. It addresses the specific challenge of engagement and adoption in a profession whose incentive structures actively discourage efficiency. And it addresses the question that sits beneath all of it: what are we actually measuring, and does it tell us what we need to know?

# AI as Both Problem and Solution

## AI as problem

What is the problem with AI with regard to training lawyers? In short, it is because the tasks AI is best at are those which previously would have been completed by junior lawyers who were training in their profession. Many of these tasks were for better or worse administrative in nature, with little opportunity to use the complex legal knowledge acquired in law school. But the cumulative effect of completing these tasks formed a vital foundation in training someone to be a lawyer. By the time juniors are advising clients they have seen multiple examples of matters and documents and understand how they fit together.

For decades, junior lawyers learned by doing work that AI is now beginning to perform: document review, due diligence, basic contract analysis, research memoranda. These tasks were not merely busywork. They were the mechanism through which new lawyers developed legal judgment, work ethic, attention to detail, and familiarity with how transactions and disputes actually progress. A first-year associate who spent months reviewing documents in a data room learned not only what a representation and warranty looks like but how a deal fits together. The tedium had a purpose.

AI eliminates much of that tedium. If AI does the due diligence, the first-year associate no longer has the opportunity to learn through doing it. This is a genuine problem, and it is not solved by simply handing the associate an AI tool and telling them to supervise.

One of the authors recalls their first work while training at a law firm. They were asked to proofread a contract. It was a simple contract, and an easy way for a senior lawyer to gauge the abilities of a junior. The author marked up the entire document, changing many “boilerplate” (read: standard template) clauses that are worded in a particular way because of years of legal precedent. They received a heavily marked up version from the senior lawyer who had put a red pen through all of their comments except those about the operative part of the contract. The author learned their lesson and never did it again.

The question those in the profession are asking now are how lawyers will navigate the nuances of complex legal practice without those formative experiences. If an AI system produces the first draft of changes to a document, where is the critical thinking required to understand if those are good changes? If the AI is drafting clauses then is the skill of legal writing stunted when something new is required? Will too much reliance on AI lead to lawyers trusting it and causing harm to a client when it is incorrect? What happens if the system fails and the lawyers don’t have any of the skills earned by doing the hard hours?

These are not challenges that are unique to law. Every profession is currently grappling with how to train juniors in a world where AI is most appropriately used by senior professionals who can verify its output very quickly. How will the juniors of today become the seniors of the future without that on-the-job training and critical thinking?

It may well be that there is no issue; that this is the way that all work will be completed in the future and people need to move with the times. In much the same way that no one needs to know how to manually file security in loan arrangements it could be that no one needs to know how to do some of the administrative tasks that will be taken by AI. Because these tools are so widespread in their use cases, though, most commentators agree that the profession needs to find some way to plug the gaps that can arise in foundational skills and critical thinking. 

## AI as solution

AI is the impetus for revamping the training model, but it is also the solution to the problem it creates. This is not a paradox. It is a design opportunity. 

The solution, however, is also AI. Firms can create simulation environments that replace learning-by-doing with learning-by-simulating. A junior lawyer can run simulated M\&A negotiations against an AI opponent, learning deal strategy, negotiation tactics, and the rhythm of a transaction in compressed time. They can use AI to practise drafting, receiving immediate feedback on their work without waiting for a partner’s redline. They can use AI as a mentor, asking the questions they are afraid to ask a real partner, building understanding through iteration rather than through the fear of looking incompetent. \[Put in examples of professors creating digital avatars through AI and how partners could do the same\]

The AI mentor is worth dwelling on. One of the most consistent themes in conversations with junior lawyers is the anxiety of asking questions. In a profession that equates competence with confidence, admitting that you do not understand something carries risk. AI eliminates that risk. A junior lawyer can query an AI tool to get the lay of the land, to understand the basic framework of an issue, and then use that as a ladder to ask smarter, more targeted questions of the humans around them. The AI does not replace the human mentor. It makes the human mentoring relationship more productive by ensuring the junior arrives at the conversation with enough context to benefit from it.

## **Law Schools and the Changing Apprenticeship Model**

Law schools face a fork in the road. They can no longer be purely academic institutions divorced from the realities of practice, or if they can, the profession must build a bridge between the academic education and the practical demands of the work.

One model suggests that law schools should integrate hands-on technical training alongside traditional legal education. Students should graduate not only understanding the law but understanding how to use the tools that will define their practice. This model has the advantage of immediacy: graduates arrive at firms ready to work with AI tools from day one.

An alternative model, more closely aligned with the investment banking apprenticeship model, suggests that law schools remain as they are: academic institutions focused on legal reasoning, statutory interpretation, and the foundations of the profession. The practical training then happens at the firm, in a structured apprenticeship that precedes independent practice. In the United Kingdom, this model already exists in the form of mandatory training contracts for newly qualified solicitors. In the United States, the transition would be more dramatic, but the principle is the same: separate the academic from the practical and structure each deliberately.

Whether or not law schools evolve, firms will need to invest in structured early-career development. If newly minted lawyers are learning practical skills at the firm rather than at school, difficult questions follow. Are they making the same salaries as current first-year associates? Are clients paying for their time? For how long? These are not rhetorical questions. They have direct implications for firm economics and for how the early years of legal practice are structured.

Meanwhile, the competition for influence over the next generation of lawyers is already underway. Harvey and Legora are taking their rivalry to law schools, offering tool-specific training and building relationships with students before they enter the profession. The vendors understand that the tools students learn on will shape the tools they advocate for as associates. This turf battle will influence which platforms dominate in firms over the next decade, and it means that law school training is no longer a purely academic question. It is a commercial one.

## **From Years to Levels: Accelerated Progression**

The traditional associate progression model, first year, second year, third year, tied to seniority and time served, is overdue for reconsideration. AI does not merely change what associates do. It changes how quickly they can develop.

The emerging model is competency-based rather than time-based. Instead of first year, second year, third year, firms will increasingly adopt level 1, level 2, level 3 designations tied to demonstrated capability rather than calendar time. Associates accelerated by AI simulation and training can reach substantive competency in months rather than years. They will not spend three years doing due diligence and e-discovery. They will work through foundational tasks quickly, demonstrate their capability, and begin doing real substantive work far sooner.

This acceleration cuts both ways. For associates who thrive, the path to meaningful work becomes dramatically shorter. For those who lack the judgment, analytical capacity, or work ethic that the profession demands, the assessment happens much faster. Where traditional progression allowed weaknesses to be masked by the sheer volume of routine work, the accelerated model surfaces capability gaps quickly. Firms will be able to determine within months, rather than years, whether an associate has the qualities needed to progress.

Associates themselves will also self-select more quickly. A junior lawyer who realises within six months that the work is not what they want to do for a living has a different career calculus than one who makes the same realisation after three years. The accelerated model is more honest, if also more intense.

The implications for firm economics are significant. If associates reach partnership-track capability faster, the traditional leverage model changes. Fewer junior associates doing more substantive work, but at higher rates, replaces large teams of juniors doing routine work at lower rates. This connects directly to the business model questions discussed in Chapter 3\.

## **Leadership Training: The Knowledge Gap at the Top**

An anecdote illustrates the problem. A first-year associate at a large firm wanted to use the firm’s AI chatbot to clean up a witness transcript. They had typed it nearly verbatim from a recorded interview and wanted to use AI to make it more readable. They told their supervising senior attorney what they intended to do. The senior attorney did not see a problem.

In a litigation context, using AI to revise a witness transcript from what was actually said to a cleaned-up version raises serious ethical and evidentiary questions. The substance of what the witness said matters. Changing it, even to improve readability, risks altering meaning in ways that could affect the case. The problem was not the junior lawyer’s initiative. The problem was that the senior lawyer did not know enough about the tool, or about the implications of its use in that context, to identify the risk.

This anecdote makes two points. The first is that partners and supervising attorneys must understand proper and improper use cases for AI tools, even if they do not use those tools themselves every day. They do not need to be power users. They need to know what the tools do, where they excel, and where their use is inappropriate or dangerous. The second point is broader: this is not a technology implementation project. It is change management for how the firm works.

Leaders need to be trained not only on tools but on change management itself. Lawyers are not, as a group, natural change agents. They are trained in precedent, in stability, in the careful management of risk. Asking them to champion new ways of working requires more than a demonstration. It requires equipping them with the skills and confidence to drive behavioural change across their teams. If the partners and supervising attorneys are not championing these new ways of working, nothing will stick.

## **Teaching Judgment, Not Just Technology**

The core challenge in AI training is not teaching people how to use a tool. It is teaching judgment: when to use which tool, when to use tools in tandem, when a task still needs manual intervention despite being automatable, and when a tool’s output needs to be overridden rather than accepted.

This challenge is compounded by a dynamic that both vendors and firm leadership have created. Harvey, Legora, and similar platforms aspire to become the operating system of the law firm: a single environment through which all legal work flows. This aspiration is understandable from a commercial perspective. It is also appealing to firm leadership, which is attracted to the simplicity of one system that handles everything. As one observer noted, the appeal is the same as an ERP: one system to solve all problems. The Clio-Vlex acquisition is an expression of exactly this logic.

But the aspiration is also a significant miss. These platforms have limits. They excel at certain types of work and are inappropriate for others. A disputes team cannot route all e-discovery work through Legora if the firm also uses Relativity or sits work in Opus. Contractual work that requires deep, jurisdiction-specific analysis may need tools that the general platform does not offer. The point solution is not dead. It remains essential for work that requires specialised capability.

Training must therefore address not only how to use a given tool but when the value of that tool ends and when the necessity of alternative tools, including point solutions, manual processes, or simply human judgment, remains. This requires a level of sophistication in training that goes well beyond the typical product demonstration. It requires ongoing education about the evolving capabilities of each tool, the relationships between tools, and the judgment to know which tool fits which situation.

## **Engagement and Adoption: The Incentive Problem**

Getting a licence is just the beginning. Real adoption requires addressing the incentive structures that drive lawyer behaviour, and those structures are, in many cases, actively hostile to adoption.

One firm reported that after a thorough rollout of a sophisticated AI tool, associates would not use it until they had hit their billable hour targets for the month. Only after they had met their personal billing requirements would they have time to explore new ways of working. This is the perverse incentive problem in its purest form. The billable hour rewards time spent, not efficiency achieved. An associate who uses AI to complete a task in two hours instead of ten has produced the same work product but billed eight fewer hours. Under traditional compensation models, that associate has performed worse, not better.

This problem is well understood, and yet it persists. It persists because the billable hour, for all its flaws, remains the dominant metric for evaluating associate contribution. Firms have begun experimenting with alternatives. Some grant associates credit for time spent using or developing AI tools, counting it as billable contribution. Others offer monetary incentives tied to adoption milestones. Ropes & Gray, for example, credits first-year associates with up to four hundred hours of AI-related work that counts toward their billable targets. The message is intentional: using AI is not a distraction from fee-earning work but a core component of professional development. \[CITATION NEEDED: Ropes & Gray AI credit programme details\]

But the problem extends beyond associates. Partners face their own version of the incentive misalignment. If a partner’s practice generates significant revenue from work that AI can now perform faster or eliminate entirely, that partner has a financial disincentive to adopt. The same logic that prevents associates from using AI until they have hit their hours prevents partners from championing tools that might reduce their practice’s billings.

## **Measuring ROI: Short, Medium, and Long Term**

The question of return on investment in AI tools is asked constantly and answered poorly. The reason it is answered poorly is that firms are asking the wrong question, or rather, asking the right question at the wrong time horizon.

In the long term, the biggest return on investment is that the firm stays in business. This is not hyperbole. The pace of change in the legal market is such that firms that do not invest in AI capability today risk being uncompetitive within three to five years. Their clients will begin to question why they are paying for manual processes that competitors have automated. Their best associates will leave for firms that offer modern tools and modern ways of working. Their market position will erode not dramatically, but steadily, in ways that become progressively harder to reverse. At this moment in time, investing in tools like Harvey and Legora is overhead, the price of doing business. It is not going to earn the firm money in the short term. The ROI discussion around these tools may therefore be fundamentally miscalculated, because it measures the wrong thing. These are not revenue-generating investments. They are survival investments.

In the medium term, the return comes from two sources. First, the firm wins more work because it operates more efficiently. Clients choose firms that can deliver faster, more thoroughly, and at competitive prices. Efficiency is not just a cost-saving exercise; it is a competitive advantage in winning mandates. Second, the firm can command higher rates for the work that remains. When AI handles the routine, the work that still requires human expertise is, by definition, the most valuable work. It is the work that clients cannot do themselves, even with their own AI tools. It is forward-looking analysis, strategic judgment, complex negotiation, and the insurance of professional accountability.

One client, in conversation with the authors, put it this way: in the future, she expects to bring her law firm a full AI-generated draft and ask only for verification and insurance. She is happy to pay a premium hourly rate for that service because the total cost is a fraction of what the full process previously required. If the rate is ten thousand dollars an hour, but the engagement takes one hour instead of fifty, the client is paying dramatically less while the firm’s effective hourly rate has increased dramatically. The highest reported hourly rate in the market at the time of writing is approximately four thousand dollars. In a few years, the logic of expertise-and-insurance billing suggests rates will go considerably higher, at least as measured per hour, even if total engagement costs decline.

The framing that emerges is this: firms are no longer selling process. They are selling a productised combination of technology, expertise, and the institutional infrastructure to stand behind the answer. That infrastructure, the firm’s data, its people, its reputation, its insurance, is what makes the service valuable. It is not the AI tool itself. It is what the firm builds around it.

## **The How: Culture, Change Management, and Local Adaptation**

For large firms, each office and each practice group is, functionally, its own little business. It has its own culture, its own leadership, its own client relationships, and its own way of doing things. What works in one office does not necessarily work in another. What engages one practice group leaves another cold.

This means that generalised adoption programmes, a single training deck rolled out firm-wide, often fail. Successful adoption requires local champions who understand the culture of their practice group and can adapt messaging accordingly. It requires experimentation with different approaches: gamification for some groups, competition for others, fear of being left behind for others still. The change management principles are well established, but their application must be tailored to the specific culture of each team.

The deeper question, debated extensively during the transcript discussions for this chapter, is which needs to change first: the incentive structure or the engagement approach? If the billable hour model actively discourages adoption, does it need to change before engagement programmes can succeed? Or can engagement drive enough momentum to force the incentive structure to evolve?

The honest answer is that it is unclear, and there is no monolith. Different firms will reach the tipping point from different directions. Some will change incentives first and watch adoption follow. Others will build adoption momentum despite the incentives and use the resulting data to justify incentive reform. The two shifts may converge gradually, and then all at once, like falling asleep, as one contributor memorably observed. What is clear is that both must eventually align. A firm cannot sustain AI adoption indefinitely within an incentive structure that punishes the behaviour it is trying to encourage.

Firms must reward two things explicitly. First, mindset change and experimentation. Some firms are giving associates credit for time spent exploring AI tools, but the same must happen for partners. If experimentation is valued, it must be valued at every level. Second, firms must begin measuring contributions in terms other than hours. Output quality, matter outcomes, client satisfaction, innovation contributions, these are all measurable. They are harder to measure than hours. But the profession will not make the transition by measuring only what is easy.

## **Looking Ahead**

Training, engagement, and adoption are not one-time exercises. They are ongoing processes that evolve as the tools evolve, as the profession evolves, and as client expectations shift. The firms that treat adoption as a project with a start and end date will find themselves perpetually restarting. The firms that treat it as a continuous discipline, embedded in how the firm operates rather than layered on top, will build durable capability. A recurring theme throughout this chapter is that artificial intelligence cannot be treated as an add-on to existing systems.

If AI is introduced as an optional tool used occasionally by a few enthusiasts, it will remain peripheral to the profession. Training programmes, law school curricula, and firm development structures must instead integrate AI into the ordinary course of legal work.

The most successful organisations will not be those that possess the most advanced tools. They will be those that embed those tools into their culture, their workflows, and their approach to learning.

Training, engagement, and adoption are therefore not temporary initiatives. They are continuous processes that evolve alongside technology and the profession itself.

## **Chapter 6**

### **Training, Engagement, and Adoption**

There is no innovation without adoption. A firm can purchase the most advanced artificial intelligence tools available, design a thoughtful strategy for their use, and invest heavily in infrastructure. None of that effort matters if the people within the organisation do not actually use the technology in their day-to-day work.

This chapter therefore looks at what happens after the technology decisions have been made. It considers the practical challenge of training lawyers, engaging them in new ways of working, and sustaining that change over time. It also examines something that must occur much earlier in the process: how the legal profession prepares its next generation of lawyers when artificial intelligence is reshaping the very tasks through which junior lawyers traditionally learned their craft.

Training, engagement, and adoption are not peripheral issues in legal innovation. They are central to it. Anyone who has worked in legal technology implementation will know that the difficulty is rarely the tool itself. The difficulty lies in persuading highly trained professionals, operating in an environment of intense time pressure and risk management, to change the way they work.

Lawyers are not natural early adopters. Their professional instincts are to interrogate risk, to rely on precedent, and to avoid unnecessary uncertainty. These instincts are valuable when advising clients, but they can slow the adoption of new tools and processes. The profession has also lived through waves of “transformational” legal technology that promised more than it delivered. Skepticism, therefore, is not irrational.

Against that backdrop, training must operate at several levels simultaneously: within law schools, within firms, and among the leaders who shape the profession. Each level plays a role in ensuring that artificial intelligence becomes embedded in the ordinary practice of law rather than existing as a novelty or a bolt-on.

## **AI as Both Problem and Solution**

Artificial intelligence presents the profession with a genuine training dilemma. For decades, junior lawyers developed their skills by performing tasks that were repetitive but educational. Document review, due diligence, first drafts of contracts, and basic legal research formed the apprenticeship through which judgment was gradually developed.

These tasks were often tedious, but they served an important purpose. A junior associate who reviewed thousands of contracts during a transaction learned the anatomy of a deal. A trainee who sifted through evidence in litigation learned how disputes unfold in practice. The work built pattern recognition and attention to detail. It was, in effect, the practical laboratory of legal education.

AI now performs many of these tasks more quickly than a human lawyer. When due diligence can be completed by a machine in minutes rather than weeks, the opportunity for learning through repetition disappears.

This creates a problem for the traditional apprenticeship model. If junior lawyers no longer perform the tasks through which earlier generations learned, how do they develop professional judgment?

The answer may lie in using the same technology that caused the disruption.

AI systems can create simulated environments in which junior lawyers practise legal work in compressed time. Negotiation exercises can be run repeatedly against AI counterparts. Drafting exercises can be assessed instantly, providing feedback that previously required hours of partner review. Litigation scenarios can be simulated so that trainees experience the strategic decisions that arise in real disputes.

In other words, AI allows the profession to move from learning-by-doing to learning-by-simulation.

The advantage of simulation is not merely speed. It also creates a safe environment for experimentation. A junior lawyer can make mistakes in a simulation without risking a client relationship. The learning loop becomes faster and less intimidating.

A particularly interesting development is the emergence of the “AI mentor”. Junior lawyers often hesitate to ask basic questions for fear of appearing uninformed. An AI system removes that social barrier. A lawyer can ask the tool to explain a procedural rule, outline the structure of a transaction, or summarise the commercial context of a matter before raising questions with a supervisor.

The AI does not replace human mentorship. Instead, it allows the human mentor to spend time on higher-level guidance rather than foundational explanation. The conversation between senior and junior becomes more productive because the junior arrives with context.

## **Law Schools and the Changing Apprenticeship**

The shift in training requirements inevitably raises questions about the role of legal education.

Law schools now face a strategic decision. Should they remain primarily academic institutions focused on legal reasoning and doctrine, leaving practical training to the profession? Or should they integrate technical and technological skills directly into their curriculum?

The answer may differ between jurisdictions.

In the United States, law schools traditionally emphasise doctrinal education. Students graduate with strong analytical training but often acquire practical skills only after joining a law firm. Clinical programmes and internships attempt to bridge the gap, but the transition to practice remains substantial.

In England and Wales, the system has historically separated academic legal education from vocational training through professional courses and training contracts. More recently, the introduction of the Solicitors Qualifying Examination (SQE) has altered this landscape. The SQE creates a centralised assessment of competence while allowing greater flexibility in how candidates acquire the experience required to qualify as solicitors.

In theory, this flexibility could democratise entry into the profession. Candidates can qualify through various forms of qualifying work experience rather than through a traditional training contract.

In practice, however, concerns have emerged about whether the system may inadvertently create a two-tier structure. Students who can afford additional preparation courses, specialist SQE training programmes, or supplementary “top-up” learning opportunities may enter the profession with significantly stronger practical skills than those who rely solely on the minimum pathway.

The risk is not merely educational inequality. It is professional inequality. If some candidates arrive at firms having trained extensively in legal technology, AI-assisted research, and digital workflows, while others have had no exposure to these tools, the gap between them may widen rapidly.

For law schools, this raises a broader question about curriculum design. Artificial intelligence cannot be treated as a niche subject offered as an optional module at the margins of a degree. Nor can it be confined to a single course labelled “legal technology”.

If AI is going to shape how law is practised, then it must be integrated into the ordinary study of law.

A contract law class might incorporate exercises in AI-assisted drafting. A civil procedure course could involve simulated litigation supported by automated discovery tools. Legal research assignments might require students to compare traditional databases with AI-driven analysis systems.

The objective is not to turn law students into technologists. It is to ensure that technology forms part of the intellectual environment in which legal reasoning takes place. When AI is embedded in the curriculum rather than added as a bolt-on, students graduate with a natural familiarity with the tools that will shape their professional lives.

This approach aligns closely with the broader Law+ philosophy that the legal profession requires multidisciplinary awareness rather than isolated expertise. Lawyers do not need to build the technology themselves, but they must understand how it interacts with the practice of law.

## **From Years to Levels: Rethinking Early-Career Progression**

If AI accelerates learning, the traditional model of associate progression may also change.

Historically, law firms have structured career advancement around years of service. A lawyer progresses from first-year associate to second-year associate and so on, largely according to time spent in practice.

Artificial intelligence challenges this model because it accelerates exposure to substantive legal work. If simulations and AI-assisted training allow a lawyer to acquire experience more quickly, the justification for strictly time-based progression becomes weaker.

A competency-based system may therefore emerge.

Instead of measuring advancement by years, firms could assess lawyers against demonstrated capability. Associates might progress through levels defined by skills, judgment, and independence rather than time served.

This model has advantages. Talented lawyers who learn quickly are not artificially slowed by rigid timelines. At the same time, capability gaps become visible earlier. A system that measures competence rather than seniority provides clearer signals about readiness for responsibility.

The implications for law firm economics are significant. If fewer junior lawyers are required to perform routine work, firms may operate with smaller cohorts of associates who undertake more substantive tasks earlier in their careers.

Such changes are already visible in some sectors of professional services. The legal profession may soon follow.

## **Leadership Training and the Knowledge Gap**

Training cannot focus solely on junior lawyers. One of the most significant barriers to responsible AI adoption lies at the senior end of the profession.

Partners and supervising attorneys do not need to be expert users of every technology. They do, however, need a working understanding of what those tools can and cannot do.

Consider a simple example. A junior lawyer might use an AI tool to tidy the wording of a witness transcript after typing it from an interview recording. On the surface, this may appear harmless. Yet in a litigation context, altering the language of a witness statement could change its meaning or raise evidentiary concerns.

If the supervising lawyer does not understand the implications of using AI in that context, the risk may go unnoticed.

Training therefore needs to include leaders as well as juniors. Senior lawyers must understand where AI tools are appropriate, where they introduce risk, and how their teams should use them responsibly.

More broadly, leaders must develop change-management skills. Lawyers are accustomed to analysing problems but not always to leading behavioural change within organisations. Successful adoption requires partners who actively champion new ways of working rather than treating technology as someone else’s responsibility.

## **Teaching Judgment Rather Than Buttons**

A common mistake in technology training is to focus on functionality. Lawyers attend demonstrations showing which buttons to press or which prompts to enter into a system.

This approach rarely produces meaningful adoption.

The real challenge is not learning how to use a tool but learning when to use it.

Modern legal practice increasingly involves an ecosystem of technologies rather than a single platform. Large generative AI systems such as Harvey or Legora aspire to become the “operating system” of the law firm, handling a wide range of tasks within a unified environment.

This ambition is understandable. Both vendors and firm leaders are attracted to the simplicity of a single system that solves multiple problems.

The reality, however, is more complex. Different tools excel at different tasks. E-discovery may still rely on specialised platforms. Contract lifecycle management may require dedicated software. Data analysis, document automation, and regulatory monitoring each have their own ecosystems.

The “point solution” is therefore far from obsolete.

Training must reflect this complexity. Lawyers need to develop judgment about which tools are appropriate for which tasks and when human intervention remains essential. They must also understand how different technologies interact with each other in a workflow.

This is less about technical mastery and more about professional literacy in technology.

## **Engagement, Incentives, and the Billable Hour**

Even the best training programmes will fail if the profession’s incentives discourage adoption.

The billable hour remains the dominant measure of lawyer performance in many firms. Yet efficiency technologies reduce the time required to complete tasks. This creates an obvious tension.

An associate who completes a task in two hours using AI instead of ten hours manually has delivered the same result with greater efficiency. Under a pure billable-hour system, however, that associate appears less productive.

Some firms have begun experimenting with ways to address this problem. Time spent learning or developing AI tools may count toward billable targets. Associates may receive explicit credit for technology experimentation.

For example, certain firms have credited substantial hours toward training and AI-related work as part of professional development programmes. The message behind such initiatives is clear: learning to work with AI is not extracurricular. It is part of the job.

Partners face their own version of the incentive challenge. If a significant portion of a partner’s practice involves work that can be automated, the partner may feel economically threatened by the technology.

Addressing this issue requires a shift in how firms measure contribution. Client outcomes, quality of advice, innovation initiatives, and long-term client relationships may become as important as raw billable hours.

## **Measuring Return on Investment**

The question of return on investment frequently arises when firms consider AI adoption.

The difficulty is that the most important benefits do not appear immediately.

In the short term, many AI tools behave like overhead. They require licensing fees, training programmes, and time spent experimenting with new workflows.

In the long term, however, the return is more existential. Firms that fail to develop technological capability may find themselves unable to compete with those that do.

Clients are increasingly sophisticated users of technology themselves. They are beginning to generate draft contracts, analyse legal risks, and conduct preliminary research using their own AI systems. When they instruct external counsel, they may bring an AI-generated document and request verification rather than creation.

In such scenarios, the law firm’s value lies not in performing routine processes but in providing expertise, judgment, and professional accountability. The service becomes a productised combination of technology, knowledge, and institutional infrastructure.

The AI tool itself is not the product. The product is the expertise that surrounds it.

---

## **Culture, Change Management, and Local Champions**

Large law firms are not monolithic organisations. Each office and practice group operates with its own culture, leadership style, and client relationships.

For that reason, adoption programmes rarely succeed when implemented as uniform, firm-wide initiatives. A single training deck circulated to every team seldom changes behaviour.

Effective change tends to emerge through local champions. These are lawyers who understand the culture of their practice group and can demonstrate practical benefits of new tools within that specific context.

Different teams respond to different motivations. Some may respond to competition. Others respond to efficiency gains or client expectations. Successful adoption programmes recognise these differences and adapt accordingly.

Ultimately, technology adoption in law is not simply a technology project. It is an organisational transformation.
