## **Chapter 3**

### **Strategic considerations: pricing, revenue, and the future of the firm**

Now that we are acquainted with why generative AI matters to law and how it might affect the work that lawyers do, this chapter turns to a more fundamental question: how does generative AI impact the underlying strategy of a law firm or legal team? Put another way: what big decisions does generative force those who work in the legal profession to make? 

If generative AI were simply a better tool, the strategic implications for law firms would be modest. Firms would buy software, train lawyers, and move on. But generative AI is not simply a better tool. It possesses a general-purpose capability that will inevitably reshape how work is priced, how value is articulated, how \- and how many \- people are recruited into the firm, how their time and work is rewarded, and the ways firms decide which markets they can profitably serve.

Importantly, when we talk about the strategic considerations of generative AI, we are **not** talking about an AI strategy. Or at least we are not talking about an AI strategy that sits separately from the wider strategy. There are, of course, necessary considerations about procurement, deployment, effective adoption mechanisms and measurement, for example, that may sit within a plan, but the cascading effects of generative AI have to be embedded into the direction of the organization. It would be impossible to consider changes to pricing, talent, workflows, and the types of work that will be delivered using generative AI without factoring that into the broader direction of the business. How to use generative AI is not a technology decision but a commercial one, as it is a development that is likely to have the most impact on the previously unshakeable billable hour \- one way or another. 

What is clear is that although firms and legal teams are experimenting enthusiastically with AI tools, far fewer are confronting the strategic consequences of what those tools make possible. Generative AI does not simply accelerate existing work. Generative AI makes some work faster, some work cheaper, and some work possible that was not previously viable at all. Each of those outcomes has strategic implications, and they do not all point in the same direction. It destabilizes long-standing assumptions about pricing, leverage, value, and, crucially, what a law firm is actually selling.

### **From time to outcomes: pricing in an AI-enabled world**

#### Pricing for value

Few topics generate as much anxiety in law firms as pricing. The billable hour has survived not because it is elegant, but because it is administratively simple. It provides a common unit of measurement across practice areas, jurisdictions, and levels of seniority. For decades, it has been the default proxy for value.

Alternative fee arrangements (AFAs) are not new. Fixed fees, capped fees, and contingency fees have existed for decades. Technically any arrangement that is not the standard billable hour counts as an AFA. Over recent years, there has also been the rise of effective fee arrangements (EFAs). EFAs base their pricing on the value of the output of the provision of legal services rather than the time it took. This allows law firms and their clients to find a happy medium between a fee that reflects the expertise required to carry out the solution and meet the needs of the client, without the disincentive to efficiency that the billable hour gives. 

Finishing matters faster is not just a case of cost-saving either; for in-house teams it will represent the freedom to more quickly proceed with whatever legal matter is in play, whether that is settling a legal dispute so that work may continue, or completing a transaction or closing sooner so business as usual can proceed. Put simply, time has reversed value for the client. The faster you deliver, the more value you create, particularly for publicly traded companies where timing in the market is critical and management attention is finite. The longer a transaction drags on, the harder it becomes for the business to operate normally. 

Historically, many AFAs have been adopted defensively. Although AFAs and EFAs are not new concepts for the legal profession, they do tend to be more prevalent in transactional practices in Europe and Asia. This is not because firms embraced outcome-based pricing of their own accord, but because clients demanded cost certainty and firms absorbed the risk through write-downs and write-offs to defend against client ire. In the States, however, especially in the BigLaw market, outcome based pricing is not yet the norm. What *is* new is the way generative AI changes the economic logic beneath fee considerations for clients \- globally. 

Generative AI changes that risk profile from defensive to tactical. Tasks that were once unpredictable become more consistent. The uncertainty introduced by a changeable volume of documents or manual processes starts to fade away. First-pass document reviews, selective reviews, and issue spotting can now be carried out with greater consistency, particularly in high-volume contexts such as due diligence. This has happened more rapidly in jurisdictions where due diligence is a distinct, scoped exercise. In the US, where deal size and liability concerns differ, the same approach has been slower to take hold. The size of the deal still matters when pricing matters, but certainty now matters more. Even there, however, the conversation has shifted from *whether* AI can be used to *where* it should be used and *how much certainty* it introduces.

Some clients have pushed their counsel further still. In the UK, a small number of institutional clients have begun experimenting with credit-based fee structures that tie payment directly to innovation and knowledge-sharing \[*citation needed*\]. The model works roughly as follows: fees are paid up to a negotiated tariff cap, after which the firm enters a credit zone. Fees accrued in that zone are only payable if the firm has delivered agreed extras over the course of the year, whether that is an innovation project, a secondment, or a knowledge-sharing initiative. If the firm has not delivered on those commitments, the client is entitled to withhold payment entirely for the overage.

Whether this model spreads widely or remains confined to a handful of sophisticated buyers is an open question. What matters is the underlying logic. Clients (in certain jurisdictions) are no longer simply negotiating rates downward but are rather redesigning the structure of the engagement itself and embedding expectations about technology, innovation, and value directly into the commercial terms. The fee arrangement is being asked to do more than account for hours worked. As noted, in the US, where the willingness to pay for time remains more entrenched, particularly on large-scale transactions, this kind of restructuring has not yet taken hold in the same way. But for firms operating across jurisdictions, the divergence should be instructive if not a harbinger. What looks like a UK-specific development may well be an early signal of where the broader market is headed.

Legal teams were never buying time. That is not why they engage their panel law firms. What clients are buying is confidence. They are paying for the firm’s judgment about where risk lies, how review should be structured, and what *not* to look at. And, if we’re being fully honest, they are paying for the security of the law firm’s insurance should things go awry. If we concede that clients buy confidence, then charging for outcomes makes much more sense. Charging for results rather than time forces firms to articulate what “additional value” actually means. Cost savings alone are rarely the full story and benefits of faster deals like accelerating time to market, freeing up management attention, and fast-forwarding commercial decisions still accrue even if the overall fee were to broadly remain the same. The strategic question for firms is whether they are prepared to price for that value explicitly.

#### **AI-enhanced pricing**

Much like with training \[see Chapter \[6\]\], the general purpose applicability of generative AI means that it not only presents new challenges but also that it provides solutions to those challenges. Although the method of pricing differs from law firm to law firm, part of the reason that firms are unwilling to shift away from the billable hour is that it is tried-and-true and has worked for years. As Richard Susskind said, it is hard to tell a group of millionaires that their business model is wrong \[*citation needed*\]. While some teams use technology to support pricing decisions, AI and its ability to parse large sets of data in natural language provides the ability to divide up the essential elements of a matter like never before. Matter codes, hours, and narratives can be uploaded to present options for average prices for phases, and these charges can be reviewed against the proportions of written down or written off time from law firms to better identify the “value” of any phase of a matter. 

\[What tools are available for this?\] \[*citation needed*\]

The implications extend beyond internal pricing decisions. As clients become more sophisticated in how they evaluate legal spend, firms that can demonstrate data-driven pricing will have a meaningful competitive advantage in winning and retaining work. A firm that can show a client, with specificity, how a proposed fee was calculated, what the historical range has been for comparable matters, and where the firm expects to deliver efficiency gains is having a fundamentally different conversation than a firm that quotes a rate and hopes the client does not push back. This is also, critically, another argument for the data strategy discussed in \[Chapter 5\]. Clean, structured matter data is not an abstract good. It is the foundation on which defensible pricing rests. A firm that cannot pull accurate historical data on how long a phase of work typically takes, how much of that time was written down, and what the effective realization rate was for similar matters is flying blind when it quotes a fee. AI can surface these patterns, but only if the underlying data is reliable. Firms with messy billing narratives, inconsistent matter coding, and fragmented time records will find that their AI-generated pricing models are only as good as what they are built on. The firms that have invested in data hygiene will be able to price with confidence, justify those prices to increasingly skeptical clients, and adapt their fee structures as the market demands. Those that have not will continue to price by instinct and hope that instinct \- and the collections \- hold.

### **Charging for knowledge, not technology** 

One of the most striking shifts in recent conversations has been the way clients talk about what they are *not* willing to pay for. There is growing resistance to the idea of being charged for “technology”, particularly where that technology feels generic. At the same time, there is a clear willingness to pay for embedded expertise.

This distinction has precedent. In eDiscovery, clients historically paid third-party vendors directly. When law firms brought eDiscovery in-house, clients continued to pay, albeit sometimes at a reduced rate, because the service remained visible and familiar. Due diligence, by contrast, never carried a separate hosting or infrastructure cost. Clients were not used to paying for the mechanics of review, only for the lawyers’ output.

Generative AI brings this tension into sharp relief. Firms that attempt to charge explicitly for tools often struggle, especially as more and more in-house teams procure the same technology. Firms that frame their offering as access to institutional knowledge, professional judgment, and insurance coverage find the conversation easier. Value billing in this sense becomes about providing an answer backed by professional confidence and accountability. In one recent example, a client developed a bespoke tool for withholding tax review and asked all of its panel firms to use it as part of the transaction workflow *\[citation needed\].* The tool generated outputs and the client knew the outputs were good. And yet the instruction to the firms was clear: we still need you to check it, because we need your insurance.

This gets to the heart of where the value proposition of law firms may ultimately settle. The client was not paying for the analysis itself. They had already done it. They were paying for the professional accountability that sits behind the analysis, for the ability, if something goes wrong, to look to the firm and its professional indemnity cover rather than to an internally-built tool and the people who built it. Even in a world where clients are technically capable of doing more of the work themselves, the demand for external legal assurance may not diminish. It may simply change shape.

This also explains the growing interest in subscription models, bespoke builds, and modular offerings. Some elements of a firm’s knowledge can be packaged and reused. Others are inherently client-specific, drawing on prior matters, playbooks, and accumulated experience. The strategic challenge is deciding what should be general, what should remain bespoke, and how far a firm is prepared to let that knowledge travel outside the firm.

### **Advising clients on AI as a distinct service line**

Even as some, if not many, rote tasks get swept in-house, more clients may turn to their counsel for advice on how to deploy AI within their own legal operations. The logic is understandable. Law firms, or at least those firms that have invested seriously in AI adoption, have spent the last two years navigating procurement, data governance, change management, and risk. These are precisely the issues that in-house teams are now confronting for the first time.

This creates two distinct but related revenue opportunities. The first is product-like: giving clients access to a tool or workflow the firm has built, often on a subscription or licensing basis. The second is advisory: helping the client think through its own AI strategy, evaluate vendors, and make decisions about where and how to deploy technology. The two are connected, but they require different capabilities, different pricing, and different conversations. A firm that conflates them risks undervaluing both. And a firm that ignores the advisory opportunity altogether may find that the clients who once relied on it for legal judgment are now also looking elsewhere for strategic guidance on technology, a conversation that, if lost, may not come back.

### **Innovation as a route to new markets**

Much of the early narrative in law has focused on cost reduction. Efficiency matters, particularly in a market under sustained fee pressure, but efficiency alone is rarely transformative in the long-run. The more strategically interesting question is what new markets become accessible when delivery costs fall. 

Generative AI does not only reduce cost. It also opens markets that were previously uneconomic. Several firms are already using AI-enabled workflows to move down-market, taking on smaller matters at scale and profit. The logic is straightforward: if the marginal cost of delivery falls, the addressable market expands.

This is where we encounter a fork in the road between the evolutionary and the revolutionary. The evolutionary approach looks at AI through the lens of the existing business: doing the same work faster, charging less to win more, and reducing non-billable downtime between matters. That is valuable, but it is also “looking under the streetlight.” The revolutionary lens asks: what entirely new capability does AI create? \- that is, work that was never previously viable, in markets that were never previously addressable. The firms that thrive will be those that can do both simultaneously.  

Some of the firms moving fastest are doing so not by building alone, but by partnering with vendors to co-develop. A certain subset of those firms are then making those co-developed products or modules available down-market to their peer (competitor) firms. This raises difficult questions about proprietary advantage. How much of a firm’s “secret sauce” can be codified without eroding differentiation? In practice, the answer often lies in what the workflow is pointed at. Generic modules may be widely usable, but the real value emerges when they are combined with client-specific data, prior deal history, and contextual judgment. In other words, it is likely less about the workflow itself and more about the knowledge that fuels it. \[*Citations needed: footnote \-* Ropes & Gray DownMarket Diligence?; Paul Weiss building workflows with Harvey; Allen & Overy’s Antitrust workflow\]

There is a strategic opportunity here for firms with uneven coverage. A strong central workflow library from a vendor can allow a firm to support smaller or emerging practice areas, where the technology acts as a thought partner where deep lawyer bench strength does not yet exist. 

A final note: there is a further dimension to these partnerships that firms would do well to consider carefully. When a firm co-develops a workflow with a vendor, the knowledge flows in both directions. The firm gains a capability tailored to its practice. The vendor gains something arguably more valuable: deep insight into how elite lawyers actually work. Every workflow co-developed with a firm teaches the vendor what matters in that practice area, what the decision points are, how experienced lawyers think through problems, and what good looks like. That knowledge does not stay locked inside the partnership. It may inform the vendor's product roadmap, its generic offerings, and ultimately the tools it sells to every other firm in the market. Firms that choose to co-develop and keep the resulting workflows proprietary may preserve a temporary advantage, but they should be clear-eyed about the fact that the underlying intelligence has already been absorbed. Firms that co-develop and publicize their work make a different bet: that the reputational and market-positioning benefits of being seen as a leader outweigh the risk of competitors learning from the example. Both strategies are defensible. What is not defensible is co-developing with the belief that the knowledge shared with a vendor remains exclusively yours. Legal technology vendors are, by the nature of what they do, aggregators of institutional intelligence across dozens of the world's most sophisticated law firms. The firms building with them are not just buying a product, they are also contributing to one.

### **The billable hour, revisited**

Predictions about the “death of the billable hour” are not new, and they remain overstated. Hours still matter because they remain the most practical way to understand the economics of a matter. Real estate, technology, and talent are largely sunk costs. Firms still need to know how much time was invested and how much money came out.

What generative AI disrupts is not the existence of the billable hour, but its dominance as the sole measure of contribution. Used uncritically, hours create a reverse incentive. Efficiency is punished. Experimentation is discouraged. The faster you work, the less you appear to contribute.

Firms are responding in different ways. Some are introducing contribution hours or credit systems that recognise time spent developing or using AI. Others are experimenting with explicit incentives, including bonuses tied to adoption or usage. Ropes & Gray, for instance, now credits first and second year associates with up to 400 hours of AI-related work that counts toward their billable targets \[*citation needed*\]. The message is clear: Using AI is not a distraction from fee-earning, but a core part of professional development. For partners, the picture is more complex. Greater efficiency may justify higher hourly rates, even as total hours fall. In some cases, fewer people may deliver more work, but at a premium. 

The firms that will be best positioned in five years are not the ones currently maximising every billable hour. They are the ones that have made deliberate room for experimentation, even at a short-term cost. That means accepting that some portion of lawyer time will be spent testing tools, refining workflows, and building institutional knowledge that does not immediately convert into revenue. It means, in some cases, funding an innovation function or an AI lab that operates outside the billable model entirely. None of this is free. It requires a willingness to absorb a near-term reduction in profits per partner, or at least to reframe how the partnership evaluates return. A firm that measures success solely by this year's PPP will always struggle to justify investment in capabilities whose payoff is two or three years away. But a firm that treats current profitability as the only metric worth protecting is making a bet, whether it realizes it or not, that the market will remain static long enough for that approach to hold. The evidence increasingly suggests otherwise. Firms that invest now in understanding how AI changes their work, their pricing, and their client relationships will compound that knowledge over time. Those that wait until the pressure is unavoidable will find themselves trying to build in a year what their competitors built over five.

### **Revenue, reduction, and opportunity cost**

A recurring mistake in AI strategy is treating it purely as a cost-reduction exercise. Efficiency matters, but efficiency alone does not create advantage. What matters is what you do with the capacity you free up.

If AI allows work to be completed faster, firms face a choice. They can charge less and win more work, reducing non-billable time between matters. Alternatively, they can redeploy that capacity into higher-value advisory work, product development, or new markets. Either way, the relevant metric is opportunity cost, not hours saved.

This requires firms to be honest about profitability by practice area. Some practices are highly exposed to automation. Others are less so. A litigation support function that relies on high volumes of junior review time faces a very different calculus from a regulatory advisory practice built on senior judgment. Data can be used to rank where investment in AI will have the greatest strategic impact, taking into account not only margin but also future risk. A practice that is profitable today but structurally vulnerable to automation in three to five years needs attention now, not when the revenue starts to decline.

Preparing for extreme futures, even those that seem unlikely, is often the best way to remain resilient whatever happens. A firm that invests in data hygiene, workflow automation, and flexible pricing structures because it believes disruption is imminent will find itself better positioned even if disruption proves gradual. The converse, a firm that assumes continuity and invests accordingly, is far more vulnerable if that assumption turns out to be wrong. The range of plausible futures for the legal profession is wider than it has been in living memory, and that uncertainty alone is a reason to act rather than wait.

### **From assessment to action: a framework for strategic prioritization**

Knowing that opportunity cost matters is one thing. Knowing where to start is another. Not every practice area will be equally affected, and not every investment will yield the same return. The challenge is to develop a structured way of deciding where to focus, one that accounts for both the urgency of the disruption and the commercial importance of the work at risk.

Some firms have begun to develop structured frameworks for exactly this kind of analysis. The approach typically involves two overlapping assessments. The first is practice-level: interviewing partners and senior associates to understand, in concrete terms, what percentage of the work within each practice area is susceptible to automation, and how quickly that change is likely to arrive. What do associates spend their first two years doing? How much of that is pattern-based? How much requires genuine judgment? The answers produce a measure of exposure grounded in the experience of the people doing the work, not in vendor marketing materials.

The second assessment is commercial: ranking each practice area by profitability, revenue contribution, and strategic importance to the firm. A small practice group generating modest revenue may not warrant significant AI investment, regardless of how exposed it is. A core profit driver facing even moderate disruption demands immediate attention.

The value of combining these two assessments is that it forces a conversation the partnership might otherwise avoid. If a practice area is both highly exposed and highly profitable, the firm cannot afford to wait. If it is exposed but marginal, the conversation is different. And if it is profitable but not yet exposed, the question is whether that will remain true, and what preparation looks like if the answer is uncertain. Is there a 50% chance that this work looks fundamentally different in three years? If so, what would we do about it? And if the probability is only 10%, does the severity of the outcome still justify preparing?

The framework does not produce a single answer. It produces a prioritized set of questions, and that is often more useful than a prescriptive plan in a landscape that is changing this quickly.

### **Why there cannot be a standalone AI strategy, revisited**

We opened this chapter by arguing that AI strategy cannot exist in a vacuum. It is worth returning to that point now, because the practical implications are hopefully now clearer.

Clients are beginning to ask two very specific questions. The first is concrete: how much money will you save me by doing this task with AI? The second is comparative: what is the delta between how long this work took you manually and how long it takes you with AI? These are not abstract enquiries. They are the beginning of an A/B testing exercise that, once completed at scale, will produce data with profound consequences for how firms are structured, how they hire, and potentially how the partnership model itself operates.

Consider what happens when a firm can answer those questions with precision. The data will show that some categories of work are susceptible to Jevons Paradox \[*citation needed*\]: AI makes the work faster, which means there is more of it, because clients who previously could not justify the cost now can. Contract review that once took forty hours and cost accordingly might now take four, making it viable for smaller deals, more frequent reviews, and broader coverage. The total volume of work may increase even as the per-unit cost falls. Other categories of work may go in the opposite direction entirely. Due diligence, for instance, may become so automated that it ceases to be chargeable as a standalone service. It becomes a cost of doing business, built into the fee for the transaction rather than billed separately. And still other categories will shift because clients, equipped with the same AI tools as their law firms, bring more and more of the routine work in-house. What they come to the firm for, as we have noted, is the knowledge, the judgment, and the insurance that AI cannot provide on its own.

Each of these outcomes points in a different direction for the business. One suggests hiring more people to handle greater volume at lower margins. Another suggests hiring fewer people and charging differently. A third suggests restructuring the firm's offering around expertise rather than process. No firm will face only one of these futures. Most will face all three simultaneously, across different practice areas, different client segments, and different jurisdictions. And that is precisely why an AI strategy cannot stand apart from the firm's strategy. The technology has reached a point where it does not merely support the business. It informs the direction the business is about to take. How a firm hires, how it structures its teams, how it prices, how it decides what work to pursue and what work to walk away from, all of these decisions are now entangled with the capabilities that AI provides. A firm that treats AI as a technology initiative managed by the innovation team, separate from the conversations happening in the boardroom about growth, hiring, and market positioning, will find that the two strategies diverge until one of them becomes irrelevant. They must grow in parallel, because in practice they are no longer two strategies at all. They are the same strategy, seen from different angles.

### **Tools without transformation**

One final point before we close this chapter, even though it will be explored in greater depth later on. Tools are meaningless without the cultural and organizational conditions that allow them to be used well. A firm can invest in the most sophisticated AI platform on the market, price it into its fee arrangements, and build workflows around it, and still fail to realize the strategic value if the people expected to use it are not equipped, incentivized, or culturally permitted to do so. The gap between procurement and adoption is not a technology problem. It is a human one, and it is where many of the strategic ambitions discussed in this chapter will ultimately succeed or fail. The chapters that follow will examine this in detail: the skills required, the training that works, the governance that enables rather than restricts, and the change management that turns a software procurement into a genuine shift in how a firm operates. But it belongs here, at the strategic level, because no pricing model, market expansion, or revenue opportunity discussed above can be realized without it.
