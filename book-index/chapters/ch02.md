# Chapter 2

## **How AI Works – and Why Lawyers Should Understand It**

For years, those in the world of legal technology have encountered questions about whether lawyers should code. The advent of generative AI shifted this question slightly to whether lawyers should be prompt engineers. Every time there is a major technological shift, the immediate burden seemingly falls on whether lawyers should become experts in it while also being experts in the law. The short answer is that while it is unlikely every lawyer will \- or should \- become an expert, they must understand what the technology does and how it affects and intersects with their work. It has become a common refrain that lawyers will not be replaced by AI, but will be replaced by another lawyer using AI. 

Artificial intelligence tools are increasingly embedded in everyday legal work, from research platforms to document review systems to productivity assistants and review tools. Headlines proclaim its ability to draft contracts, summarise case law, predict litigation outcomes, and even build software tools. Lawyers do not need to become computer scientists in order to use these tools effectively. However, a basic understanding of the underlying technology helps ensure that it is used appropriately, safely, and creatively. To understand how generative AI works, it is useful to know its origin, abilities, and shortcomings.

But where to start? Despite rapid adoption and quickly becoming one of the most discussed technologies in the legal profession, there remains considerable misunderstanding about what artificial intelligence actually is and how it works. This leads to lawyers either not using AI correctly, not using it at all, or expecting it to do things for which it is not best suited and being disappointed when it cannot. All of these are factors that stop AI being fully integrated into how law is being practised. 

This chapter therefore provides a brief and practical introduction to the technical foundations of artificial intelligence and generative AI. It explains how the technology evolved, how modern systems operate, and why emerging concepts such as agents and “vibe coding” are beginning to reshape the way lawyers interact with technology. 

### **A brief history of artificial intelligence**

#### Artificial intelligence: the early years

Artificial intelligence is often presented as a recent breakthrough, but the idea itself is not new. Researchers have been attempting to build machines that mimic human reasoning for more than seventy years.

The field began formally in the 1950s when computer scientists started exploring whether machines could replicate aspects of human intelligence such as logic, reasoning, and problem solving. The idea of mapping technology like a human brain is known as a “neural network.” Early systems relied on what are known as *rule-based* approaches. In these systems, human experts would encode knowledge directly into the computer in the form of rules: if a certain condition occurred, the program would respond in a predetermined way. Think of it like a series of “if, then” statements; if a person selects option X then the system will produce outcome A and if they select option Y then the system produces outcome B. At their core, these systems were advanced “choose your own adventure” novels. These early attempts produced interesting demonstrations but struggled to scale. The world is too complex to be captured entirely through explicit rules, especially for law firms who are attempting to introduce technology into complex decision-making processes. Many a legal technologist has had to act as a translation intermediary between developers requiring a strict set of steps to build into the technology and the lawyers whose answer is “it depends”. As a result, interest in AI rose and fell in cycles often referred to as “AI summers” and “AI winters”.

A major shift occurred with the rise of **machine learning**. Rather than programming computers with explicit rules, researchers began training models to identify patterns within data. By analysing large datasets, these systems could learn statistical relationships and use them to make predictions or classifications.

Machine learning enabled technologies such as spam filters, recommendation systems, and fraud detection tools. Many of these systems were already widely used in legal technology long before the recent wave of generative AI.

The latest phase of development emerged from advances in **natural language processing** and the availability of enormous datasets and computing power. Researchers developed models capable of analysing language at scale and predicting the relationships between words and concepts. When these models grew sufficiently large, they began demonstrating an ability to generate coherent text, answer questions, and perform complex reasoning tasks. This started with simple things like predicting the next word. Think about when you are drafting a text message and your phone suggests the next word you should use. Of course, if you keep just predicting the next word without considering what came before then what you eventually end up with is a bunch of nonsense. 

#### Transformers and LLMs

This is where **transformers** came in. What transformers allowed these tools to do is to consider much more context all at once. Instead of just looking at the next appropriate word, transformers allowed the tools to look back at entire sentences and paragraphs to understand the context of the words and to predict what should come next. 

One useful way to understand why transformer models matter is to consider a simple word that lawyers – like everyone else – encounter frequently: “train.” The word itself has several completely different meanings depending on context. It could refer to:

* a locomotive;  
* physical training such as going to the gym;  
* educational training;  
* training a machine learning model; or  
* a sequence of events (as in “a train of thought”).

Humans resolve these differences almost instantly because we naturally process the context around the word. If someone says:

“I missed the train this morning.”

We immediately picture a railway station rather than a gym session. But computers historically struggled with this type of ambiguity. Earlier language models often processed text in a more limited, sequential way. They might analyse only a small number of nearby words or rely on predefined rules about language.

Consider the sentence:

“I need to do training tomorrow.”

Without sufficient context, a computer might assume the sentence refers to exercise. After all, in everyday language the phrase “to train tomorrow” frequently relates to going to the gym or practising a sport.

Now consider a longer sentence:

“I need to do training tomorrow because the new model isn’t performing well.”

Here, the meaning changes entirely. The sentence is referring to training a machine learning model, not physical exercise. Transformer models approach language differently. Instead of processing text strictly word by word, they analyse all the words in a sentence together, identifying how each word relates to the others. This mechanism is known as “attention”.

When a transformer encounters the word “train”, it does not treat it in isolation. Instead, it examines the surrounding words and asks which ones are most relevant to interpreting the meaning.

Consider the sentence:

“The passengers waited on the platform for the train.”

When analysing the word “train”, the model pays attention to words such as *passengers* and *platform*. These strongly suggest that the word refers to a locomotive.

Now compare it with:

“She plans to train at the gym tomorrow morning.”

Here the words *gym* and *tomorrow* provide very different signals, indicating that *train* refers to physical exercise. Because transformers analyse the entire sentence simultaneously, they can weigh these contextual clues and arrive at the correct interpretation. 

#### Tokens and context windows

To understand why generative AI behaves the way it does, it is helpful to examine some of the technical concepts that underpin the technology. Large language models process language not as words but as tokens. A token is a small unit of text that may represent a word, part of a word, or even punctuation.

When a prompt is entered, the model converts the text into tokens and processes them as numerical representations. The model then predicts the next token in the sequence based on the probability distribution it has learned during training. Without getting too technical, they essentially:

* split up words (and portions of words) into chunks;   
* encode them into a unique “barcode” (the token) representing that chunk;   
* compare that barcode against the other encoded chunks of text being reviewed (and against the huge amount of words it has already ingested as part of its training); and  
* predict and then produce the next chunk of text.

This ability to interpret context across entire sentences, paragraphs – and even across entire documents – is one of the reasons large language models have become so capable at working with human language. Legal documents are full of terms whose meaning depends heavily on context. Words such as *consideration*, *execution*, *charge*, or *assignment* have everyday meanings but very specific legal ones. Transformer architectures allow AI systems to examine surrounding language and infer which meaning is most appropriate in the situation.

In other words, the model is not simply recognising words. It is analysing relationships between words across a piece of text, allowing it to generate responses that reflect the intended meaning of the language being used. These systems are now known as large language models (LLMs), and they underpin many of the generative AI tools currently being deployed across the legal profession. 

One concept to bear in mind on top of the concept of tokens is the “context window.” This refers to the amount of text that the model can consider at one time when generating a response. If a model has a context window of 100,000 tokens, it can analyse roughly that amount of text when producing an answer. This is what enables modern AI tools to review long documents or sets of documents when asked to summarise them.

However, the context window is not infinite. If a conversation becomes too long or too much information is included, earlier material may fall outside the window and effectively be forgotten by the model. This limitation has practical implications for legal workflows, particularly when analysing large datasets or complex matters that involve many documents.

### **Hallucinations**

#### 

Generative AI can sometimes appear almost magical. A user types a question or instruction into a chat interface and receives a detailed response within seconds. It is tempting to assume that the system “understands” the question in the same way that a human would, particularly when we anthropomorphise our tools by giving them names. Just like “Siri” and “Cortana,” the legal profession has “Harvey.”

In reality, the underlying mechanism is far more statistical than cognitive. Large language models are trained on vast collections of text drawn from books, articles, websites, and other sources. During training, the model learns to predict the most likely next word in a sequence given the words that came before it. This may sound simple, but when repeated billions of times across enormous datasets the model learns subtle relationships between language, concepts, and contexts.

When a user enters a prompt, the model does not \- for the most part \- retrieve an answer from a database or search engine. Instead, it generates a response token by token, selecting each new word based on probabilities derived from its training. 

One of the most widely discussed limitations of generative AI is the phenomenon often referred to as “hallucination”. A hallucination occurs when the model generates information that appears plausible but is factually incorrect or entirely fabricated. This behaviour arises from the probabilistic nature of language models. Because the system is generating text based on statistical patterns rather than verifying facts against an authoritative source, it may occasionally produce outputs that are internally consistent but incorrect.

For lawyers, this issue is particularly important. Legal work depends on accuracy, verifiable sources, and careful interpretation of authoritative texts.The solution is not to avoid AI altogether but to recognise its role. Generative AI is often best used as a drafting and analytical assistant, producing initial outputs that are then reviewed and verified by a professional. In other words, it should be treated as a powerful tool rather than an autonomous advisor.

### Connecting AI to real information

Above it was mentioned that generative AI tools are trained on general datasets and do not automatically have access to specific information such as a law firm’s internal knowledge base or a client’s documents. To overcome this limitation, many systems use techniques such as **retrieval-augmented generation (RAG)**. In this approach, the system first searches a dataset or knowledge base for relevant information. The retrieved material is then provided to the language model as part of the prompt so that it can generate a response based on those sources.

For example, a legal research assistant might retrieve relevant cases or legislation from a database before generating a summary for the user. This approach reduces the risk of hallucinations and ensures that the model’s output is grounded in specific documents.

It also highlights an important principle: AI is only as useful as the data it can access. In the early days of generative AI deployment within law firms, many lawyers asked internal AI tools questions about firm financials, client matters, or internal precedents. These tools could not answer because they were not connected to those datasets. Users had inadvertently attributed far more knowledge to the system than it actually possessed. An early example of this was encountered by one of the authors: when asking the legal generative AI tool the questions “How does Harvey arrive at its answers,” the tool proceeded to give an answer that did not reference itself \- or any generative AI \- at all. What it did instead was give an overview as to why the Batman villain Two-Face, whose alter-ego is Harvey Dent, made his decisions on the flip of a coin. Harvey did not have access to the requisite data to understand that *it* was Harvey.

### Probabilistic vs. Deterministic

Understanding what data an AI system can and cannot access is therefore essential. This process is often described as stochastic, meaning that it involves probabilities rather than deterministic rules. If the same question is asked multiple times, the model may produce slightly different answers because multiple responses could plausibly follow the prompt. To manage this complexity, the model relies on a network of weighted connections between billions of parameters. These weights represent the strength of relationships between different linguistic patterns learned during training. The potential problem is that these tools are not necessarily designed to *be* correct but to *sound* correct. 

For lawyers accustomed to working with structured sources such as statutes and precedents, this probabilistic approach can be counterintuitive. The model is not verifying facts against an authoritative source. It is generating text that is statistically likely to follow from the prompt. Consider this in an area such as document automation. Generative AI models are increasingly being used to fill in templates or to automate documents. Without knowing how the software is working, a lawyer might rightfully wonder why they are paying for a document automation tool while also paying for generative AI which seems to do it in a similar manner. However, the probabilistic nature of generative AI means that it won’t always produce the same results, which can be necessary in structured legal documents and templates. 

In writing about this area, Catherine Bamford of Bam\!Legal \[request permission\] states that lawyers will use a combination of generative AI and more traditional deterministic automation: “AI to extract info, context and intent,” and “deterministic logic to produce something consistent, trusted and risk free”.

This explains both the strengths and the limitations of generative AI. The technology excels at drafting, summarising, and synthesising information because it has learned patterns across enormous bodies of text. However, it can also produce incorrect or fabricated statements if the statistical pattern suggests a plausible continuation that is not factually accurate. Understanding this distinction is essential when deploying AI tools within legal practice.

### **Model types and capabilities**

Not all AI systems operate in the same way. Broadly speaking, modern AI tools can be divided into several categories depending on their purpose.

**Predictive models** are designed to analyse data and forecast outcomes. In a legal context, this might include systems that estimate litigation risk or predict the likelihood of certain contractual clauses appearing in a document.

**Classification models** categorise information based on patterns learned during training. Document review platforms frequently use this approach to identify privileged materials, relevant documents, or particular clause types, for example for a due diligence exercise.

**Generative models**, by contrast, create new content. These include systems capable of drafting text, generating summaries, producing code, or answering questions in conversational language.

Many modern tools combine these approaches. A legal AI platform might first classify documents, then summarise them, and finally generate insights for the user.

### **The emergence of AI agents**

As generative AI tools become more capable, a new concept has emerged in the field: AI agents. Most early generative AI systems functioned as simple conversational interfaces. A user would submit a prompt, receive an answer, and then decide what to do next. The system itself did not take independent actions and was unable to perform a series of actions or access a variety of sources.

Agents extend this model by allowing AI systems to perform tasks across multiple steps and tools. As was defined in Chapter 1, “agents” are autonomous software systems powered by generative AI \[globally refer to Generative/generative AI in the same way\] that can navigate between different environments in order to achieve a task. Rather than answering a single prompt, an agent can plan a sequence of actions in order to achieve a defined goal.

For example, a legal research agent might:

* interpret a user’s question;  
* search a legal database for relevant cases;  
* retrieve and analyse those documents; and  
* produce a summary or draft memorandum.

Each step may involve interacting with external systems such as databases, document repositories, or scheduling tools. In practical terms, agents move AI from being merely a source of answers to being a **participant in workflows**. Instead of simply generating text, the system can coordinate tasks and manage processes.

For legal teams, this opens the possibility of AI systems that support end-to-end activities such as due diligence reviews, regulatory monitoring, or knowledge retrieval.

Model Context Protocol

Frameworks such as Model Context Protocol (MCP) are emerging to enable these interactions in a structured way. Instead of simply generating text, the system can coordinate tasks, retrieve information, and produce outputs that support complex processes.

For legal teams, this opens the possibility of AI systems that support activities such as:

* due diligence reviews

* regulatory monitoring

* knowledge retrieval

* contract analysis

* internal research requests

### Model Context Protocol (MCP)

As AI systems become more integrated into professional workflows, one challenge quickly emerges: how does the AI interact with the tools and data that professionals actually use? A generative AI model on its own is powerful at producing text, but it does not automatically know how to access a document repository, search a legal database, or retrieve information from a firm’s knowledge management system. In order to perform these tasks, the model needs a structured way to communicate with external systems. This is where the Model Context Protocol (MCP) comes in. 

In simple terms, MCP is a standardised way for AI models to connect to tools, applications, and data sources. It provides a framework that allows the model to request information or perform actions through clearly defined interfaces. Rather than building a bespoke integration for every possible system, developers can use MCP to create a consistent way for AI systems to interact with external services. 

In many ways, MCP performs a role similar to APIs (\[define\]), the standard connectors that allowed earlier generations of software to integrate with each other. The difference is that these connectors are now designed specifically for AI systems that need to interpret user intent and decide which tools to use dynamically. 

To understand why this matters, it helps to think about how lawyers already work with software. A lawyer drafting a document might use several different systems in the course of a single task. They may search a legal database for a precedent, retrieve documents from a document management system, check internal knowledge resources, and then draft text within a word processing tool. Each of these systems contains valuable information, but historically they have existed as separate tools with a manual burden on the lawyer to navigate between them. 

Generative AI introduces the possibility of bringing these systems together through a single interface. However, for that to work, the AI needs a way to access and use those systems safely and reliably. MCP provides that mechanism. 

Under the protocol, external systems expose their capabilities as tools that the AI model can “call” (interact with) when needed. These tools might include functions such as searching a database, retrieving a document, sending a message, or running a calculation. Each tool has a defined description that explains what it does and what inputs it requires. When a user asks the AI to perform a task, the model determines whether it needs to use one of these tools. If so, it sends a request through the MCP interface, receives the relevant data or result, and incorporates that information into its response.

Imagine a lawyer at RJM LLP asking an AI assistant the following question:

“Perform a company search against Company A and produce a company search checklist.”

In order to answer this request effectively, the AI system may need to perform several steps. It might retrieve the latest version of the company search checklist from the document management system, search the relevant company search repository for the jurisdiction in question, download the latest relevant company documentation, and then analyse the company’s information before inserting it into the company search checklist format..

Without a structured way to connect to those systems, the model would not be able to access the relevant information. Stages might have been integrated by using APIs but APIs only allow specific tasks to occur so in our example the company search website might not have a way to download in its API. With MCP, each of those resources can be exposed as tools that the AI can call as part of the workflow.

An important feature of MCP is that it separates the intelligence of the model from the systems it interacts with. The model focuses on interpreting the user’s request and deciding which tools are needed. The tools themselves handle the interaction with the underlying systems and data.

This separation brings several advantages. Firstly, it improves security and control. Organisations can define exactly which tools are available to the AI system and what information those tools can access. The model does not gain unrestricted access to internal systems.

Secondly, it improves flexibility. New tools can be added over time without changing the underlying model. If a firm deploys a new research database or knowledge platform, a corresponding MCP tool can be created to allow the AI to interact with it.

Finally, it allows AI systems to become more useful in practical workflows. Instead of operating purely as conversational assistants, they can begin to interact with the same systems that professionals use every day. Picture an MCP flow triggered by the receipt of an email containing “Company Search Checklist” in the subject line as a simple exmaple.  

For lawyers, the practical implication is that AI tools will increasingly move beyond simply generating text. They will begin to retrieve documents, analyse data, and interact with internal systems as part of a broader workflow. When combined with the concept of agents, protocols such as MCP make it possible for AI systems to carry out complex tasks involving multiple steps and tools. The model can interpret a user’s goal, select the appropriate tools through MCP, and assemble the results into a coherent output. This increasing ability to cope with complexity and ability to undertake complex tasks led to the emergence of “vibe coding” in law.

### **What is vibe coding?**

While the term may sound informal, it reflects an important shift in how software is being created. This topic is reviewed in more detail in Chapter \[ \], but traditionally, building software required writing code line by line using programming languages. This placed software development firmly in the domain of trained engineers.

Generative AI has begun to change this dynamic. Recent models are capable of generating functional code based on natural language instructions. A user can describe the functionality they want—such as a simple web application or data analysis tool—and the system can produce the underlying code required to build it. “Vibe coding” refers to this more conversational, iterative approach to software development. Instead of specifying every technical detail, the user describes the outcome they want and collaborates with the AI system to refine the result.

Traditionally, building software required writing code line-by-line using programming languages such as Python or Java. This meant that software development was primarily the domain of trained engineers. Generative AI has begun to change this dynamic. With the code being created by using natural language instructions, a user can describe the functionality they want and the system can produce the code required to build it.

For example, a user might ask an AI system to create:

* a tool that analyses contracts for specific clauses;  
* a dashboard that tracks regulatory changes; or  
* an automated workflow that organises documents for a transaction.

The system generates the code required to build the tool, which the user can then test and refine. To showcase the importance of vibe coding, one need only look at what happened when Claude \- Anthropic’s generative AI tool \- announced a Claude Legal plug-in. It affected the shares of a number of legal technology companies. \[Source\]

For lawyers and legal operations teams, this development is potentially significant. Many small tools that would previously have required months of development work may now be prototyped quickly with the assistance of AI. Internal dashboards, document workflows, or client-facing tools can sometimes be built through iterative prompts rather than extensive programming.

This does not eliminate the need for professional software engineers. Complex systems still require careful design, security oversight, and long-term maintenance. However, it lowers the barrier for experimentation. Legal professionals may increasingly find themselves able to create or prototype tools that support their own workflows.

### **What AI is actually good at**

Now the history and essential elements of the workings of generative AI are understood, the question is what the best uses of the tools are for legal practice. Generative AI performs particularly well at tasks involving **language transformation**. These include tasks like \[global change to make the way we use lists consistent\]:

* drafting and redrafting text;  
* summarising long or complex documents;  
* extracting key information from documents;  
* translating between formats or styles;  
* comparing documents for differences; and  
* answering questions based on provided material.

Because legal work is heavily language-based, many everyday tasks fall into these categories. In practice, this means AI can assist with activities such as drafting first versions of documents, reviewing large sets of materials during due diligence, producing summaries of case law, retrieving information from knowledge repositories, or preparing presentations and client briefings. These capabilities do not replace legal judgment. Instead, they accelerate parts of the workflow for providing legal advice that involve processing and organising large amounts of information.

### **Why lawyers should understand the technology**

Some lawyers may reasonably ask whether they need to understand any of this at all. After all, most professionals use technology without knowing how it works internally. There are also those more senior practitioners who wonder if they can “wait it out,” and avoid understanding AI through retirement. They cannot.

There are several reasons why a basic technical understanding of AI is valuable in legal practice. Generative AI tools are general-purpose technologies. Unlike earlier legal software that was built for a single task, many modern AI tools can be applied to a wide range of problems. This means the user often decides how the technology should be used. \[Note: AI has tendency to say First, second, etc\] In other words, the value of the tool increasingly depends on the imagination and judgment of the professional using it. 

Understanding the technology also helps lawyers assess risk and reliability. Lawyers are increasingly involved in decisions about procurement, governance, and regulation of AI systems. Clients expect legal advisors to understand both the capabilities and the risks associated with these technologies.

Even without giving this sort of advice, basic technical literacy enables more productive collaboration with the specialists who design and deploy these systems. Just as lawyers work alongside accountants, project managers, or data analysts, the future legal team will include technologists and AI specialists. A shared understanding of the underlying concepts allows for better collaboration.

### **Understanding enough to ask the right questions**

None of this means that lawyers must learn to build neural networks or develop their own end-to-end agentic workflows. The role of the lawyer remains fundamentally the same: to advise clients, interpret the law, and help solve complex problems.

However, the tools used to deliver that advice are evolving rapidly. Lawyers who understand the fundamentals of AI will be better equipped to select appropriate use cases, supervise outputs, and identify new opportunities for improving legal services. In that sense, the goal is not to transform lawyers into technologists. It is to ensure that the legal profession develops enough technical fluency to ask the right questions and apply these tools thoughtfully. 

As we will explore in the following chapter, once these fundamentals are understood the more important challenge becomes strategic: deciding where and how artificial intelligence should be deployed within legal practice.
